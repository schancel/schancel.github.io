Or: How I Learned to Stop Oscillating and Start Building Again

2026-02-10

The Grown-Ups Have Entered the Chat: From the Ruins of Metamodernism to the Laws of Apodictism

The last century of Western thought has been, to put it charitably, a very expensive learning experience about which parts of civilization are decorative and which parts prevent the roof from caving in on your head.

We are not—contrary to the hand-wringing of conservative intellectuals—living through the failure of modern philosophy. We are living through its success. It successfully demolished everything. Congratulations. The wrecking crew did exactly what it was hired to do.

The problem is that the wrecking crew is still on site, sitting in the rubble, having feelings about how deconstructed everything is, while insisting that building anything would be “problematic” because what if we accidentally recreate oppressive structures?

Meanwhile, it’s raining. And cold. And several people have died of exposure.

The grown-ups have entered the chat. We’re called Apodictists. And we’re here to do something your graduate seminar forgot was possible: distinguish between walls and cages.

The Four Ages of the House: A Brief History of How We Got Here

Modernism: The Age of the Glass Box

Modernism looked at the ornate Victorian mansion of Western civilization—all those unnecessary gargoyles and inexplicable turrets and rooms labeled “this is where we keep the sacred mysteries”—and said: “This is inefficient. Let’s build a glass box with clean lines and rational planning.”

The Modernists weren’t wrong that the Victorian mansion had problems. There were rooms built for functions nobody remembered. There were hierarchies encoded in who could use which staircase. There were a lot of “because we’ve always done it that way” justifications that didn’t survive scrutiny.

So they tore it down and built a glass box. Everything visible. Everything measurable. Everything optimized. Science would explain the universe. Economics would solve scarcity. Psychology would cure the soul. Progress was a straight line, and we were climbing it.

Humans, it turns out, don’t actually thrive in glass boxes. The clean lines were sterile. The rational planning didn’t account for things that couldn’t be measured. The straight-line progress kept hitting walls that the blueprints said shouldn’t exist.

Turns out some of those “unnecessary” Victorian features were load-bearing. We just didn’t understand what they were bearing until we removed them and watched things collapse.

Postmodernism: The Age of the Wrecking Ball

Postmodernism looked at the glass box and said: “This is a lie. The ‘neutral’ planning encodes hidden power. The ‘objective’ measurements serve particular interests. The ‘universal’ progress is parochial colonialism in a lab coat. Let’s take a wrecking ball to the whole thing.”

And they did. With enthusiasm.

Postmodernism was the greatest demolition project in intellectual history. They deconstructed everything: truth, meaning, identity, morality, beauty, reason itself. Every claim to universality was revealed as contingent. Every appeal to nature was shown to be cultural construction. Every hierarchy was exposed as arbitrary power play.

The insight was real. Power structures were hidden in claims to neutrality. Colonialism did dress itself up as objective science. A lot of what claimed to be universal truth was just one particular culture generalizing its preferences. But, postmodernism itself was not neutral.

The deconstruction needed to happen. The glass box was a lie. The wrecking ball was necessary.

However, you can’t live in a pile of bricks.

Postmodernism was brilliant at identifying what was arbitrary. It was hopeless at distinguishing between arbitrary and necessary. It could tell you that marriage was a social construct, but it couldn’t tell you whether the coordination problems marriage solved were also social constructs or whether they were structural constraints that return when you remove the solution.

Postmodernism stumbled over the load-bearing walls while demolishing the cages. It couldn’t tell the difference because it assumed all structure was arbitrary power. The stumbling stone wasn’t oppression—it was necessity they couldn’t see.

The wrecking ball didn’t discriminate. It smashed cages and load-bearing walls with equal enthusiasm, then declared victory because at least nothing was oppressing anyone anymore.

Except gravity. And entropy. And the fact that humans still need coordination mechanisms to avoid freezing to death in the rubble.

Metamodernism: The Age of the Ghost

Metamodernism looked at the rubble and said: “We can’t go back to the glass box because postmodernism proved it was a lie. But we also can’t stay in pure deconstruction because that’s uninhabitable. So we’ll… oscillate. Between ironic detachment and sincere longing. Between critique and construction. Between mourning the house and pretending we’re fine without it.”

Metamodernism is the philosophy of people who know they need a house but are afraid to build one because what if it’s problematic?

It gave us:

  • “I’m being sincere, but like, ironically sincere”
  • “I know meaning is constructed, but I’m going to construct meaning anyway, wink wink”
  • “We need grand narratives, but we can’t believe in grand narratives, so we’ll perform grand narratives while being anxiously self-aware about performing them”

You can’t go RETRVN to naïve modernism. You can’t pretend postmodernism didn’t happen. You have to somehow build while acknowledging that building is hard and previous builders fucked up.

But, oscillation is not architecture.

Metamodernism has been sitting in the ruins for two decades, feeling very sophisticated about its ability to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously, while getting progressively wetter and colder because it’s still raining and nobody has built a roof. You can’t build on sand and expect the house to stand. Shifting perspectives, oscillating positions, foundations that wash away under pressure: this is sand, not bedrock.

The metamodern condition is being too smart to believe in anything and too honest to pretend you don’t need to believe in something. It’s intellectual purgatory. High on vibes, low on floorboards.

And meanwhile, people are dying of exposure. Birth rates collapsing. Mental health cratering. Communities fragmenting. Coordination failing. All while the intellectuals congratulate themselves on their nuanced appreciation of complexity.

Apodictism: The Age of the Architect

We’re done oscillating.

Apodictism is not a return to modernism (the glass box was a lie). It’s not a rejection of postmodernism (the deconstruction was necessary). It’s not even a rejection of metamodernism (acknowledging complexity is good).

Apodictism is what comes after you’ve deconstructed everything and discovered that some things don’t deconstruct.

There are walls you can’t remove because they’re holding up the ceiling. There are patterns you can’t change because they’re encoding constraints in reality itself. There are structures that aren’t arbitrary power moves: they’re coordination solutions to genuine problems that return when you remove them. These are rock, not sand. They don’t shift with cultural opinion or intellectual fashion.

Apodictism is the observation that moral reality has structure, and that structure is discoverable, and that discovering it is not optional if you want civilization to continue functioning.

We’re not “suggesting” this would be nice. We’re observing it’s apodictically necessary and demonstrably true through logic and evidence.

The game has changed. We’re no longer asking “what should we value?” We’re asking “what are the constraints that operate whether we acknowledge them or not?”

The Age of the Architect has begun. And the architects have brought blueprints.

The Sieve Method: How to Distinguish Rubble from Foundation Stones

Here’s what metamodernism couldn’t figure out: How do you know which structures to keep and which to discard?

Conservatism says: “Keep everything old because tradition contains wisdom.” This preserves load-bearing walls AND cages. It can’t distinguish.

Progressivism says: “Remove everything old because tradition encodes power.” This removes cages AND load-bearing walls. It can’t distinguish either.

Metamodernism says: “Oscillate between both positions while being anxious about choosing.” This keeps you dry approximately 0% of the time.

Apodictism says: “Use the sieve.” Or if you prefer ancient language: winnow the wheat from the chaff. Separate pearls from ashes. Distinguish walls from cages.

The full method is in Pearls from Ashes: A Method for Discovering Moral Structure After Deconstruction. The extended application to modern relationships is in Legion, Go Into the Swine. But here’s the framework:

The sieve method has four filters. Run every traditional structure through them:

Filter 1: Observable Consequences

Does violating this structure produce predictable damage? Not social punishment (which can be arbitrary), but thermodynamic collision—coordination entropy, trust infrastructure collapse, cascading system failures. This isn’t sin. It’s engineering error. Ignoring these laws doesn’t make you immoral; it makes you wrong in the way that building a bridge without accounting for tensile strength is wrong.

Example: Reliability in relationships.

When people can’t be counted on (when their “yes” doesn’t mean yes) trust infrastructure collapses. Coordination becomes impossible. Relationships fail in predictable patterns. This isn’t social construction. It’s structural necessity. Unreliable people can’t form stable partnerships because reliability is a constraint, not a preference.

Compare: Using the wrong fork at dinner. Social punishment? Maybe. Structural damage? No. That’s arbitrary convention. Into the discard pile.

Filter 2: Invariance Across Contexts

Does this pattern hold regardless of culture, time period, or belief system? This is what engineers call the Lindy Effect—the longer something has survived, the longer it’s likely to survive, because it’s encoding solutions to persistent problems. Not everything old is wise, but patterns that emerge independently across very different contexts probably aren’t arbitrary. They’re load-bearing.

Example: Reciprocity.

Shows up in every human culture. Not because everyone copied each other, but because coordination structurally requires it. You can’t build stable relationships, communities, or exchanges without some form of “I do for you, you do for me.” Specific implementations vary (gift economies vs. market economies), but the underlying pattern is invariant.

Compare: Specific gender roles. These vary enormously across cultures and time. Some elements might be structural (biological constraints), but many are clearly cultural variation. Mostly into the discard pile.

Filter 3: Independent Validation

Has psychology, behavioral economics, game theory, or other empirical research independently discovered this pattern? When ancient wisdom and modern science converge on the same observation through completely different methods, you’re probably looking at something real.

Example: Attachment theory.

Validates that humans need secure base relationships to explore and take risks effectively. This wasn’t derived from religious texts. It was discovered through observation of children and adults across cultures. It happens to align with ancient wisdom about stable relationships and community, but the validation path is independent. Keep this stone.

Filter 4: Structural Function

Does this structure enable coordination, trust formation, or transformation that doesn’t happen without it? Not “does this serve someone’s interests” (everything does), but “does this solve a coordination problem that returns when you remove it?”

Example: Confession practices.

Structured vulnerability with trusted others enables healing from shame, accountability for behavior, and integration of hidden parts of self. Remove this structure (as many modern communities have), and you see predictable patterns: isolation, inability to process shame, lack of accountability, fragmented identity. The function is observable. The structure was solving coordination problems we didn’t understand. Load-bearing. Keep it.

What the Sieve Removes

Empty signaling. Arbitrary conventions. Pure status games. Historical accidents that encoded no functional information. Traditions that served power without solving coordination problems. Cultural specifics that don’t generalize.

Christianese terminology? Ash. Most of the words are just arbitrary tribal markers.

But the pattern of progressive character alignment those words pointed at? Pearl. That’s structural necessity validated by psychology, visible across cultures, solving coordination problems.

Victorian sexual repression that damaged people? Ash. Arbitrary control serving power.

But the recognition that sexual behavior has consequences for trust and pair-bonding? Pearl. That’s structural constraint validated by attachment research.

The Three Pillars: Apodeictic Foundations That Don’t Deconstruct

Once you run everything through the sieve, certain patterns keep showing up. Not because we want them to be true. Because they’re apodictically necessary: you can’t build functioning civilization without them.

Here are three:

Pillar 1: The Necessity of Ritual

Postmodernism was right that rituals can encode power and enforce conformity. But it missed that ritual is how humans transmit information that can’t be encoded propositionally.

Some knowledge is experiential. Some transformations require repeated enactment. Some coordination requires synchronized behavior that creates common knowledge.

Every functional culture has rituals for major life transitions—birth, adulthood, marriage, death. When secular societies removed religious ritual, they immediately invented new ones: graduation ceremonies, professional credentialing, civic celebrations. Psychology validates that regular practices shape identity and community bonds in ways one-time intellectual assent doesn’t. Attachment research shows children need predictable rhythms and repeated patterns to develop security.

The apodeictic claim: Humans require ritual structures to coordinate at scale, transmit cultural knowledge, mark transitions, and create shared meaning. The question isn’t “ritual or no ritual,” it’s “which rituals serve coordination vs. which serve arbitrary power?”

Examples that pass the sieve:

  • Liturgical calendar: Structures time around meaningful rhythms. Secular societies discovered they needed this (academic calendars, fiscal years, cultural holidays) because coordinating human activity requires shared temporal reference points.
  • Confession practices: Structured vulnerability enables processing shame and maintaining accountability in ways spontaneous conversation doesn’t.
  • Rites of passage: Identity transitions require ordeal and witness. Removing these doesn’t eliminate the transitions. It makes them illegible and unsupported.

The modernist mistake was thinking ritual was primitive superstition we’d outgrow. The postmodernist mistake was thinking ritual was pure power enforcement. The metamodernist mistake was performatively reinventing ritual while being too ironic to admit that’s what they were doing.

The apodictist observation: Ritual is structural necessity. Build consciously or watch spontaneous (usually dysfunctional) rituals emerge anyway.

Pillar 2: The Objectivity of the Aesthetic

This will make people uncomfortable, but: Beauty is not subjective.

Not “beauty” in the sense of “what any individual finds pleasing” (that varies). Beauty in the sense of patterns that enable human flourishing and coordinate perception across observers.

Certain mathematical ratios—the golden ratio, Fibonacci sequences—show up in nature and in architecture humans find beautiful across cultures. Environmental psychology shows natural patterns (fractal complexity, biophilic design) reduce stress and improve cognition. Classical architecture persists across millennia while modernist buildings age poorly and often get demolished. Music theory validates that certain harmonic structures create consonance vs. dissonance, and these patterns are cross-cultural. Facial symmetry correlates with health markers and is found attractive across all human populations.

The apodeictic claim: Beauty is structural information about order, proportion, and fit-to-function. Aesthetic patterns that persist across contexts are encoding real constraints, not arbitrary preferences.

The modernist mistake: “Beauty is whatever serves function efficiently.” This gave us brutalist architecture that humans hate and buildings that make people depressed.

The postmodernist mistake: “Beauty is whatever the dominant culture says it is, so we’ll celebrate ugliness to subvert power.” This gave us art that deliberately alienates viewers and architecture designed to make people uncomfortable.

The metamodernist mistake: “Beauty is subjective but we miss it, so we’ll create beauty while being anxious about whether we’re allowed to say things are beautiful.” This gave us… not much, actually. Mostly oscillation.

The apodictist observation: Human nervous systems respond to certain patterns consistently. These patterns are discoverable. Classical architecture worked because it encoded these patterns. Modern architecture that ignores them produces measurable harm (increased stress, reduced social interaction, cognitive load).

We’re not “preferring” classical proportions. We’re observing that they solve coordination problems (creating shared beauty standards, reducing cognitive load, enabling social cohesion) that modernist and postmodernist aesthetics fail to solve.

Build beauty into the reconstruction, or watch people flee your buildings for environments that don’t assault their nervous systems.

Pillar 3: The Reality of Hierarchy

This is where everyone gets nervous, but: Hierarchies are not arbitrary power structures. Some hierarchies are structural necessities for coordination at scale.

Every organization above Dunbar’s number (150 people) develops hierarchical structure. Attempts to create “flat” organizations either fail or secretly develop informal hierarchies that are less accountable. Developmental psychology shows children need hierarchical structure (parental authority) to develop secure attachment. Game theory validates that coordination problems require some decision-making authority concentrated somewhere. Evolutionary biology shows dominance hierarchies across social species solve coordination problems.

The apodeictic claim: The question isn’t “hierarchy or no hierarchy,” it’s “which hierarchies are functional coordination mechanisms vs. which are arbitrary domination?”

**The key distinction is that functional hierarchy authority serves coordination. Leaders are accountable to those they lead. Hierarchy solves problems (faster decision-making, clear responsibility, efficient information flow). And, arbitrary domination – power serves power-holder – has no accountability, no coordination benefit. It is Pure extraction.

The tests are straightforward. Can you leave? In functional hierarchy, yes—in domination, no. Does the hierarchy enable coordination that doesn’t happen without it? Functional hierarchy does; domination doesn’t. Is leadership accountable to those led? Functional hierarchy requires it; domination denies it. Does removing the hierarchy solve problems or create them? Removing functional hierarchy creates problems; removing domination solves them.

Examples:

  • Parental authority: Load-bearing. Children need hierarchical structure during development. “Egalitarian” parent-child relationships produce insecure attachment and poor outcomes.
  • Ecclesiastical hierarchy: Mixed. Some church structures solved coordination problems (maintaining doctrine, resource distribution, conflict resolution). Others were pure power extraction (indulgences, abuse cover-ups).
  • Professional credentialing: Mixed. Some credentialing creates costly signals that filter for competence. Some is pure gatekeeping serving existing power.

The modernist mistake: “Hierarchies are efficient management tools.” This created corporate hierarchies optimized for extraction, not coordination. The postmodernist mistake: “All hierarchy is oppression, dismantle everything.” This gave us organizations that can’t coordinate and children raised without structure who develop attachment disorders. The metamodernist mistake: “We need leadership but hierarchies are problematic, so we’ll have ‘emergent leadership’ while being anxious about whether that’s hierarchy.” This gave us dysfunction pretending to be flat while actually operating through informal power networks less accountable than formal hierarchy.

The apodictist observation: Humans require hierarchical coordination mechanisms at scale. The project is distinguishing functional hierarchy (serves coordination) from arbitrary domination (serves power-holder). This is discoverable through the sieve method.

Build conscious, accountable, functional hierarchies into the reconstruction, or watch them emerge spontaneously as informal power networks that are worse in every way.

Why This Matters: The Difference Between Oscillation and Architecture

Metamodernism has been intellectually sophisticated about its paralysis. It can hold multiple perspectives. It acknowledges complexity. It refuses easy answers.

This is admirable. It’s also insufficient.

Because while the metamodernists were being nuanced, observable consequences were piling up:

  • Birth rates below replacement (coordination failure in family formation)
  • Mental health crisis (coordination failure in meaning-making)
  • Institutional collapse (coordination failure in trust)
  • Inability to build infrastructure (coordination failure in collective action)
  • Relationship breakdown (coordination failure in pair-bonding)

These aren’t “concerning trends we should think more carefully about.” These are structural collapses with predictable cascading failures.

You can be as nuanced as you want about the complexity of building. Meanwhile, people are freezing to death in the rubble.

Apodictism is not a rejection of complexity. Complex systems are complex. Coordination is hard. Building is harder than demolishing.

Apodictism is a rejection of paralysis.

When you’ve identified load-bearing structures through the sieve method, you don’t “oscillate” about whether to keep them. You keep them because the alternative is structural collapse.

When empirical research, cross-cultural patterns, and observable consequences all point to the same coordination mechanism, you don’t “remain open to other interpretations.” You build on that foundation because it’s apodictically necessary.

This isn’t arrogance. It’s observation of constraints. And if it sounds arrogant, that’s because constraint-level truths always do when stated plainly. Gravity doesn’t apologize. Structural failure doesn’t negotiate. Neither does thermodynamic reality.

The “No Refunds” Policy

If you miss the metamodern condition – the sophisticated oscillation, the ironic sincerity, the freedom from having to believe anything definitely – you’re welcome to stay in the rubble.

Nobody’s forcing you to help build, but understand: the reconstruction is happening with or without you.

Because turns out, when it’s raining and cold and people are dying of exposure, “remaining open to multiple perspectives about whether walls are necessary” stops being sophisticated and starts being negligent.

Some of us have accepted that reliability is not a preference but a structural requirement for trust, that beauty is not subjective but discoverable pattern enabling flourishing, that ritual is not primitive but necessary for coordination and meaning transmission, that hierarchy is not inherently oppressive—functional authority serves coordination—and that moral constraints operate whether we acknowledge them or not.

We’re not “suggesting” this would be nice. We’re demonstrating it’s apodictically necessary through multiple validation paths.

You can argue about it. You can demand more nuance. You can insist that everything is more complicated. While you’re doing that, we’ll be building.

And when the roof is on and the rain stops and people have someplace warm to sleep, you’re welcome to move in. But you don’t get to complain that the walls are “problematic” because they limit your freedom to walk through where walls are.

That’s what walls do. They’re structural constraints. And structural constraints are precisely what enable the protection you’re currently missing while you oscillate about whether protection is ideologically pure enough to accept.

Welcome to Apodictism: The Laws Are Already Here

The thing about apodeictic truths is they don’t care whether you believe them.

Gravity operated before Newton described it. Thermodynamics constrained the universe before we discovered the laws. Moral structure exists whether we acknowledge it or not.

Apodictism isn’t inventing new constraints. We’re noticing the constraints that have been operating all along.

The constraints that produced predictable damage when we violated them. The constraints that wise traditions encoded before we understood why they worked. The constraints that postmodernism couldn’t deconstruct because they’re not social constructions—they’re features of reality.

The project isn’t to argue people into believing this. The project is to demonstrate it’s true through:

  • Observable consequences when violated
  • Invariance across contexts
  • Independent empirical validation
  • Structural function that doesn’t work without it

If the demonstration is correct, the structures we’re building will work better than the alternatives. They’ll enable coordination that metamodern oscillation couldn’t achieve. They’ll solve problems that postmodern deconstruction made worse.

And when that happens, you won’t need to believe in apodictism. You’ll just notice that the people building on apodeictic foundations have houses, and the people oscillating in the rubble are still getting rained on.

The Laws are already here. We’re just finally reading them.

The Age of the Architect has begun. The salvage operation is underway. The reconstruction will be built on what actually works, not what we wish worked or what seems ideologically pure.

And we are the living stones—not passive materials waiting to be placed, but active builders deciding whether to help construct something that stands or keep stumbling over necessities we refuse to see.

Pearls from ashes. Load-bearing walls from rubble. Civilization from coordination.

Welcome to Apodictism. The grown-ups have entered the chat.

Now let’s build something that doesn’t collapse.

Your First Stone

The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone. You know the feeling—there’s something you dismissed as oppressive tradition that keeps showing up in your thoughts. Some structure you threw out with the bathwater. Some pearl buried in the ashes you were too sophisticated to salvage.

Your first task: Identify one “ash” you’re clinging to (empty signaling, arbitrary convention, pure status game) and one “pearl” you’ve discarded (load-bearing structure you dismissed as problematic). Run them both through the sieve. Swap them.

Then place one stone on another. The Age of the Architect doesn’t wait for perfect blueprints. It starts with recognizing which stones don’t break under pressure, which walls actually hold up the ceiling, which structures enable the coordination you need to survive.

The Laws are already here. We’re just finally reading them correctly. And the reconstruction begins with you deciding whether you’re going to help build or keep oscillating in the rubble while it rains.
