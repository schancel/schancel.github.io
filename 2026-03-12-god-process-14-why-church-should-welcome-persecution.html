The God Process (12 of 13): Why the Church Should Welcome Persecution

The day they fired me for my faith was the day I discovered what Christianity was supposed to feel like.

For years, I’d been a comfortable Christian—showing up to church, saying the right things, living an easy faith that cost me nothing. Then my company demanded I participate in training that directly contradicted biblical truth. I politely declined. Three weeks later, I was unemployed.

But something unexpected happened in that moment of loss: I felt more alive spiritually than I had in decades.

Suddenly, my faith wasn’t theoretical anymore—it was the most real thing in my life. The comfortable Christianity that had sedated my soul for years burned away, leaving something pure and strong and unshakeable.

But here’s what amazed me: This was exactly what Scripture said would happen. “Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness.” —(James 1:2-3)

God hadn’t abandoned me in persecution—He was refining me through it, just as He promised.

Here’s what I learned that transformed my understanding of persecution: It’s not something that destroys authentic Christianity—it’s something that reveals and purifies it, exactly as Scripture promises. And the Western Church desperately needs this biblical purification process.

“He will sit as a refiner and purifier of silver, and he will purify the sons of Levi and refine them like gold and silver.” —(Malachi 3:3)

God uses opposition as His refining fire—not to destroy us, but to reveal the gold that was there all along.

Look honestly at your Christian experience: Comfortable pews. Entertainment-focused services. Sermons that comfort rather than challenge. Social Christianity that requires no sacrifice and costs nothing.

I lived this for years—and it nearly destroyed my faith through comfort addiction.

But I’ve watched persecution transform churches in ways that comfort never could. I’ve seen believers discover depths of faith they never knew they possessed when the cost of following Christ became real.

Historical patterns prove what I experienced personally: Persecution doesn’t destroy authentic Christianity—it purifies it, strengthens it, and reveals what was truly there all along.

The Purification Algorithm

Jesus declared persecution’s inevitability: “In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world.” (John 16:33)

This was not a warning—this was a computational promise of purification.

Persecution operates through these invariant mechanisms that comfort cannot replicate:

  • Eliminates counterfeit Christians through cost-benefit analysis exposure
  • Strengthens authentic faith via pressure-testing that reveals structural integrity
  • Forces priority clarification through binary choice requirements
  • Creates warrior unity among genuine believers facing coordinated opposition
  • Produces character through the only process capable of forging spiritual steel

Historical analysis suggests a consistent pattern: Extended comfort often correlates with spiritual complacency, while moderate persecution tends to strengthen authentic faith.

The Historical Data

I’ve studied church history and witnessed persecution’s strengthening effects personally:

Early Church (AD 30-313): Systematic persecution generated exponential growth, crystal-clear theology, and martyrs whose witness converted the Roman Empire. The more Rome tried to destroy Christianity, the stronger it became.

Chinese Underground Church: I’ve met Chinese pastors who spent decades in prison for their faith. Their spiritual depth and biblical knowledge put most Western pastors to shame. Communist persecution created the world’s fastest-growing Christian movement. One pastor told me, “Prison was my seminary. Suffering was my education.”

Soviet Christianity: Seven decades of state atheism produced believers with unshakeable faith. I know a Russian grandmother who said, “We had no Bibles, no buildings, no safety—so we had to have Jesus. And that was enough.”

Compare this to comfortable Western Christianity: Declining attendance, theological confusion, and Christians who can’t explain basic biblical concepts because they’ve never had to fight for their faith.

The pattern is undeniable: Opposition reveals who’s really following Christ and who’s just playing church. Persecution burns away everything fake and leaves everything real stronger than before.

The Strategic Necessity of Welcoming Persecution

Christians can gain strategic advantages by understanding persecution’s historical functions:

1. Persecution Reveals Authentic Faith (Just Like Jesus Said)

“Everyone then who hears these words of mine and does them will be like a wise man who built his house on the rock… And everyone who hears these words of mine and does not do them will be like a foolish man who built his house on the sand.” —(Matthew 7:24,26)

When I lost my job for my faith, something clarified immediately: The people who said “I’ll pray for you” but disappeared versus those who showed up with practical help and spiritual encouragement. Jesus warned that storms reveal which houses have rock foundations—persecution reveals which believers have authentic faith foundations.

2. Persecution Produces Biblical Spiritual Maturity

“But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil.” —(Hebrews 5:14)

Comfortable Christianity keeps believers on spiritual milk—dependent, weak, easily shaken. But I watched my friend Sarah face intense family opposition for her conversion. The pressure forced her to develop unshakeable biblical convictions, deep prayer life, and spiritual maturity that 20 years of comfortable church attendance never produced. She moved from milk to solid food through necessity.

3. Persecution Creates the Unity Jesus Prayed For

“That they may all be one, just as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be in us, so that the world may believe that you have sent me.” —(John 17:21)

Before opposition hit our community, churches argued about worship styles, denominational differences, and secondary theological issues. When local businesses started discriminating against Christians, suddenly those divisions disappeared. We discovered we were family, not competitors, and we needed each other to survive—exactly the unity Jesus prayed for.

4. Persecution Validates the Gospel Through Witnessed Cost

“By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another.” —(John 13:35)

Nobody listens to comfortable Christians talking about sacrifice—we have no credibility. But when my neighbor saw me lose income and social standing for Christ, then watched my family remain joyful and faithful through hardship, he started asking questions about what could be worth that cost. Jesus said people would recognize His disciples through their love—suffering for each other proves that love is real.

5. Persecution Restores Biblical Eternal Perspective

“But our citizenship is in heaven, and from it we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ.” —(Philippians 3:20)

Comfortable Christianity makes you think this world is your home and comfort is your goal. Persecution reminded me that I’m just passing through, and my real citizenship is elsewhere. Paul wrote about heavenly citizenship from prison—persecution clarifies what Scripture teaches about our true home and ultimate allegiance.

Psychological Preparation Protocols

How to Prepare for Persecution (Without Living in Fear)

Week 1-2: Normalize Biblical Christianity

Realize that comfortable Christianity is the historical exception, not the rule. For most of church history, following Christ cost something significant. My perspective shift: Instead of asking “Why is this happening to Christians?” I started asking “Why did we think it wouldn’t happen to us?”

Week 3-4: Count the Cost Like Jesus Commanded

Jesus told us to count the cost before following Him (Luke 14:28). Ask yourself honestly:

  • What will I do when my employer demands I compromise my values?
  • How will I protect my children from anti-Christian indoctrination?
  • Will I choose biblical truth or legal safety when I can’t have both?
  • Am I ready to love difficult neighbors who oppose my faith?

My discovery: Just thinking through these scenarios strengthened my resolve and reduced my anxiety.

Week 5-8: Build Persecution-Resistant Community

Individual Christians can’t survive sustained opposition alone. Start building:

  • Church relationships focused on truth rather than entertainment
  • Economic networks with other faithful believers
  • Educational alternatives for your children’s protection
  • Mutual support systems for when opposition comes

Real example: Our church network now includes Christian contractors, teachers, doctors, and business owners who prioritize serving believers. When one faces opposition, the whole network responds.

Week 9-12: Develop Unshakeable Faith Foundations

Build faith that can survive external pressure:

  • Study doctrine until you can explain what you believe and why
  • Read Scripture systematically, not just devotionally
  • Practice spiritual disciplines that develop real spiritual strength
  • Learn from church history about how believers survived persecution

The goal isn’t fear—it’s confidence. When you know why you believe and have community support, persecution becomes purification rather than destruction.
