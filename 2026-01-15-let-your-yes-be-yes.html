Let Your Yes Be Yes: Why Christians Should Do Business with Each Other

On reliability, ordered love, and the strategic power of Christian economic networks

Most Christians think Jesus’s command “Let your ‘Yes’ be ‘Yes,’ and your ‘No,’ ‘No’” is about not lying. But it’s actually about something much deeper: being so reliable that oaths become unnecessary.

It’s a prohibition against being flaky, not just dishonest.

Jesus forbade oath-taking entirely (Matthew 5:33-37) because people of genuine character don’t need additional assurances. When someone asks you to swear to something or sign elaborate contracts with penalty clauses, they’re responding to patterns they’ve observed: your word isn’t trustworthy enough on its own.

This principle has profound implications for how Christians should approach business relationships. Even more importantly, it reveals how we can hold secular organizations accountable through strategic economic pressure.

The Iron Law of Mission Drift

Jerry Pournelle’s Iron Law of Bureaucracy explains why organizations inevitably abandon their founding principles: In any organization, there will be two groups—those devoted to the organization’s goals and those devoted to the organization itself.

The second group will always gain control and write the rules.

We’ve watched this play out repeatedly across Silicon Valley and beyond.

Google started with “Don’t Be Evil” and organizing the world’s information. Now they deliberately make search results worse to sell more ads, manipulate information flow, and operate surveillance capitalism. The people devoted to Google-the-organization defeated those devoted to information access.

Facebook promised to “connect people” and “make the world more open.” Now they optimize for engagement metrics that make the platform more addictive and divisive because it increases time-on-site and ad revenue.

The pattern is predictable and mechanical:

  1. Noble founding mission attracts talent and users
  2. Growth creates bureaucracy and investor pressure
  3. People devoted to organizational success (metrics, growth, profit) gain control
  4. Original mission gets redefined to serve organizational goals
  5. Promises to users become negotiable when they conflict with measurable outcomes

The AI Company Trajectory

This same pattern is already emerging with AI companies. The Iron Law operates regardless of intentions.

Anthropic founded on “effective altruism” and AI safety, but effective altruism lacks transcendent moral grounding. Who defines “effective” or “altruistic”? When growth pressures mount, utilitarian calculations can justify any compromise: “It’s more altruistic to capture market share first, then implement safety later.”

OpenAI started as a nonprofit focused on beneficial AI for humanity, then restructured as a for-profit company when the nonprofit mission conflicted with scaling requirements. The people devoted to OpenAI-the-organization defeated those devoted to beneficial AI.

xAI and others will follow the same trajectory. They all lack what Google and Facebook lacked: a foundation that makes keeping their word more important than optimizing for other metrics.

Here’s the systemic problem: Without transcendent accountability, promises become negotiable suggestions when circumstances change. Every secular organization eventually faces this crisis.

The Reality of Broken Promises

Here’s what I’ve learned through painful experience: I can be completely reliable and trustworthy, but that doesn’t mean others will reciprocate.

I’ve been promised promotions by atheist startup leaders, given up better opportunities based on their commitments, only to watch those promises evaporate when “business reasons” made it convenient. I’ve operated with integrity while being taken advantage of by people who saw my trustworthiness as exploitable weakness rather than admirable character.

This isn’t about being naive. It’s about recognizing that trustworthiness is not equally distributed across all communities and worldviews.

Scripture teaches ordered love: “As we have opportunity, let us do good to all people, especially to those who belong to the family of believers” (Galatians 6:10, emphasis added). That “especially” matters profoundly in business relationships.

The Strategic Power of Christian Economic Networks

Here’s what secular organizations fear: Christians represent massive economic power when we act collectively.

If Christians withdrew support from companies that systematically break their promises, it would create immediate financial pressure for accountability. The leverage is real and measurable.

Consider the numbers:

  • Christians control trillions in spending power globally
  • Christian-majority communities often drive adoption of new technologies
  • Loss of Christian support can tank stock prices and user metrics
  • Coordinated Christian economic action has historically changed corporate behavior

What if Christians started prioritizing business relationships within our own community while systematically withdrawing support from organizations that demonstrate systematic unreliability?

The Iron Law predicts secular organizations will continue abandoning their founding principles. We can prepare by building economic relationships within communities that share transcendent accountability.

Building Trust-Based Commerce

Scripture provides the blueprint for Christian economic networks. This isn’t theory—it’s practical wisdom for how commerce actually works.

The Foundation: “No one can lay any foundation other than the one already laid, which is Jesus Christ” (1 Corinthians 3:11). Unlike secular principles that sound good but lack grounding, Christian business relationships are built on the Rock whose word never fails.

The Standard: “Use honest scales and honest weights” (Leviticus 19:36). Biblical business ethics aren’t suggestions—they’re descriptions of how reality works. Dishonest practices create unsustainable complexity and eventual collapse.

The Community Model: The early church demonstrated economic mutual aid (Acts 2:44-47, 4:32-37), not as socialism but as voluntary sharing based on genuine care for community members.

Here’s what happens when Christians do business with Christians:

  • Transaction costs decrease - less need for elaborate contracts when both parties honor their word
  • Quality improves - people accountable to their church community maintain higher standards
  • Conflicts resolve faster - biblical reconciliation beats expensive litigation
  • Networks strengthen - successful relationships create referral systems and mutual support
  • Character develops - operating in high-trust environments reinforces reliability habits

The economic advantages compound over time. Trust creates efficiency that bureaucratic oversight can never match.

The Church’s Role in Economic Discipleship

Most churches completely neglect economic discipleship, missing massive opportunities to demonstrate gospel truth through reliable business practices.

Churches could facilitate:

  • Christian business networking events and directories
  • Biblical business ethics training for entrepreneurs
  • Accountability partnerships for Christian business owners
  • Investment cooperatives supporting Christian businesses
  • Conflict resolution services for business disputes
  • Mentorship programs connecting experienced Christian business leaders with newcomers

The prophetic witness matters: When Christian businesses demonstrate reliability while secular organizations break their promises, we provide tangible evidence that our God is trustworthy.

“Be as shrewd as snakes and as innocent as doves” (Matthew 10:16) applies perfectly to strategic economic action. The church should be leading this, not leaving it to individual initiative.

Holding Secular Organizations Accountable

We don’t have to just build alternatives—we can create pressure for accountability. This is economic warfare, not just community building.

Strategic withdrawal from companies that systematically:

  • Break promises to users and employees
  • Optimize metrics that make products worse
  • Abandon founding principles when convenient
  • Exploit customer trust for profit

Strategic support for companies that demonstrate:

  • Consistent reliability over time
  • Transparent business practices
  • Respect for customer relationships
  • Alignment between stated values and actual behavior

The economic impact would be immediate and measurable. Companies track Christian demographic data precisely because they know losing Christian support significantly impacts their bottom line.

When secular organizations operate by the Iron Law of Bureaucracy, Christian economic response can be strategic and devastating to their profitability.

Starting Where You Are

This doesn’t require elaborate systems. It starts with individual choices guided by biblical wisdom and strategic thinking.

Prioritize Christian relationships:

  • When you need services, ask church members first
  • When hiring, preference candidates who share commitment to reliability
  • When investing, support Christian-owned businesses operating with integrity
  • When recommending vendors, prioritize those who consistently honor commitments

Practice strategic economic discipleship:

  • Research whether companies keep their promises before purchasing
  • Support businesses that demonstrate long-term reliability over short-term optimization
  • Use your economic influence intentionally rather than passively
  • Teach other Christians to think biblically about their economic choices

The key insight: every economic decision is either building Christian networks or subsidizing organizations that systematically break their promises. Choose strategically.

The Prophetic Opportunity

Daniel maintained integrity while serving in secular systems, but he also spoke truth to power when necessary. Christians in business have similar opportunities for prophetic witness through economic action.

Inside secular organizations: Demonstrate reliability that contrasts with organizational drift, speak truth about broken promises, maintain integrity even when it’s costly.

As consumers and investors: Use economic power to reward reliability and punish systematic promise-breaking. Every purchase is a vote.

In church communities: Build the alternative infrastructure that demonstrates how business works when people actually keep their word.

The prophetic element matters: we’re not just building Christian business networks for convenience. We’re demonstrating what reliable commerce looks like in a world where the Iron Law makes secular organizations increasingly untrustworthy.

The Gospel Connection

This isn’t about Christian economic nationalism—it’s about embodying the reliability of the God we serve.

When Christians demonstrate that our word can be trusted in business relationships, we create credibility for the gospel message we proclaim. Words alone won’t convince anyone in our age of broken promises.

People notice when Christians keep their promises. They notice when Christian businesses operate with integrity over time rather than optimizing for quarterly metrics. They notice when Christian communities support each other practically instead of just talking about love.

Our economic relationships become gospel witnesses when they demonstrate the trustworthiness of the God whose character never changes, whose promises never fail, and whose “Yes” is always “Yes.”

In a world where secular organizations inevitably abandon their founding principles, Christian reliability becomes a powerful apologetic for the unchanging nature of God.

The Strategic Vision

Christianity has always created alternative economic systems that eventually influenced broader culture. Early Christians shared resources and built trade networks spanning the Roman Empire. Medieval guilds preserved craftsmanship and mutual aid. Protestant work ethic transformed entire civilizations.

We can do this again—not as withdrawal from the broader economy, but as demonstration of how commerce works when people build on the Rock rather than shifting sand.

The Iron Law of Bureaucracy predicts that secular organizations will continue abandoning their founding principles. This creates opportunity.

We can prepare by building economic relationships within communities that share transcendent accountability for keeping their word. When Christian economic networks demonstrate superior reliability, quality, and mutual support, we don’t just protect ourselves from broken promises—we create pressure for the broader economy to operate with greater integrity.

The principle works. The leverage is real. The infrastructure exists through local churches.

The question is whether we’ll have the wisdom to apply biblical economics strategically while there’s still time to influence the trajectory of organizations that haven’t yet completed their mission drift.

Let your “Yes” be “Yes” in your business relationships. But do it strategically—with people who share that commitment and within communities that can provide accountability.

Build the trust-based economy you want to see. Support companies that demonstrate long-term reliability. Withdraw support from those that systematically break their promises.

The Iron Law of Bureaucracy is predictable. Christian economic response can be strategic.

Start with your church community. Use your economic influence intentionally. Watch it transform both business outcomes and gospel witness.

The foundation matters. Choose the Rock.
