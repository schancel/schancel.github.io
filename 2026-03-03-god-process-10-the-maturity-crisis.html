The God Process (8 of 13): The Maturity Crisis

After twenty years in church, I realized I was still a spiritual toddler.

I could quote Scripture, sing worship songs, and attend every service, but when real spiritual opposition hit—when my faith was tested by actual hardship, when I needed to make difficult moral decisions under pressure, when people around me needed spiritual strength I didn’t possess—I collapsed.

I wasn’t alone. Looking around my church, I saw hundreds of people who’d been “believers” for decades but functioned like spiritual children: emotionally dependent, easily manipulated, paralyzed by uncertainty, requiring constant encouragement just to maintain basic faith.

The devastating realization: Our church had spent years creating spiritual consumers instead of spiritual warriors.

About this we have much to say, and it is hard to explain, since you have become dull of hearing. For though by this time you ought to be teachers, you need someone to teach you again the basic principles of the oracles of God. You need milk, not solid food, for everyone who lives on milk is unskilled in the word of righteousness, since he is a child. —(Hebrews 5:11-13)

Paul’s words hit me like a physical blow: “By this time you ought to be teachers.” I’d been a Christian for two decades, but I couldn’t handle spiritual truth beyond the elementary level. I needed constant emotional comfort instead of being able to provide strength for others.

But then I discovered something that changed everything: Scripture doesn’t just diagnose the maturity crisis—it provides the exact training methodology that creates spiritual warriors. The same Bible that confronted my immaturity contained the blueprint for developing genuine spiritual strength.

Here’s what I’ve learned: Modern church culture systematically prevents spiritual maturity—but biblical training methods create spiritual warriors when properly implemented. And we desperately need warriors right now.

Think about your own church experience: Sunday services designed to make you feel good rather than grow strong. Small groups focused on sharing feelings instead of developing character. Sermons that comfort your weaknesses instead of challenging you to overcome them. You’ve been trained as a spiritual consumer, not a spiritual warrior.

And then real life happens. Your teenager rebels against everything you’ve taught them. Financial crisis hits your family. You face a serious illness. A family member dies unexpectedly. Your spouse struggles with addiction. You encounter systematic opposition to your faith at work.

This is where you discover the difference between spiritual children and spiritual warriors.

Spiritual children collapse: They lose faith when hardship hits. They make decisions based on emotions rather than principle. They require constant pastoral care to maintain basic Christian behavior. They abandon biblical values when facing cultural pressure. They become casualties instead of soldiers.

Spiritual warriors thrive under pressure: They use hardship for character development rather than emotional breakdown. They make moral decisions based on biblical truth regardless of personal cost. They provide stability for others during crisis. They resist cultural pressure through conviction rather than compliance. They become leaders instead of liabilities.

The difference isn’t natural talent—it’s training methodology. And Scripture provides the exact training program that produces spiritual warriors when churches actually implement it.

Spiritual development follows clear patterns observable across every domain:

The rest were spiritual children: emotionally dependent, easily manipulated by cultural pressure, paralyzed when facing uncertainty, requiring constant pastoral care to maintain basic Christian behavior. Decades of church attendance had produced emotional consumers, not character.

Modern church culture deploys four systematic approaches that create spiritual dependency:

The comfort trap: Modern church eliminates genuine hardship rather than using it for character formation. Scripture reveals God’s training approach: “It is for discipline that you have to endure. God is treating you as sons. For what son is there whom his father does not discipline?” —(Hebrews 12:7). Yet churches remove discomfort, preventing the adversarial training spiritual development requires.

The therapeutic substitution: Truth gets replaced with emotional soothing. Paul predicted this pattern: “For the time will come when people will not endure sound doctrine, but having itching ears they will accumulate for themselves teachers to suit their own passions” —(2 Timothy 4:3). Modern preaching leaves people feeling better rather than transformed—stagnation masquerading as spiritual growth.

The entertainment inversion: Formation gets substituted with consumption. Scripture describes genuine community: “Not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another” —(Hebrews 10:25). The word “encouraging” means calling alongside for strategic support, not entertainment consumption.

The complexity avoidance: Churches simplify moral decisions rather than developing discernment. Yet maturity requires practice: “But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil” —(Hebrews 5:14). This prevents the development required for spiritual maturity.

Spiritual adulthood requires three-phase development that most churches systematically prevent:

Phase 1: Basic Training (Truth Foundation). Systematic biblical literacy beyond inspirational verses. Clear understanding of fundamental beliefs. Moral framework grounded in objective truth. Prayer discipline seeking God’s will rather than personal comfort.

Phase 2: Combat Training (Character Formation). Deliberate exposure to spiritual opposition. Practice making difficult moral decisions under pressure. Learning to function effectively when emotions are disrupted. Developing independent judgment aligned with biblical principles.

Phase 3: Leadership Training (Strategic Thinking). Understanding spiritual warfare at systemic level. Strategic thinking about long-term consequences. Ability to provide stability for others during crisis. Vision for advancing God’s kingdom rather than personal comfort.

Here’s what broke my heart: Most churches never progress beyond Phase 1—and many don’t even complete that. When cultural pressure mounted against biblical values, they responded with clear conviction rather than emotional reactivity. When families faced crises, mature believers provided real spiritual strength instead of just sympathy.

Most importantly: Their young adults don’t abandon faith in college because they’ve been trained to handle intellectual and spiritual opposition rather than just consume religious comfort.

The contrast with typical church members is dramatic—and it proves spiritual maturity is achievable when churches actually prioritize character formation over entertainment.

Your Path to Spiritual Maturity Starts Now

If you’re tired of being a spiritual child, here’s what worked for me:

Week 1-12: Truth Foundation (Biblical Basic Training) “All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work.” —(2 Timothy 3:16-17)

Stop consuming inspirational content and start systematic biblical study. Read through the Bible asking “What does this teach me about God’s character and my responsibilities?” not “How does this make me feel?” Develop consistent prayer discipline focused on seeking God’s will and aligning with His purposes. I spent 30 minutes daily for three months just learning basic theology—it transformed how I understood everything about God, myself, and spiritual warfare.

Week 13-24: Character Training (Spiritual Boot Camp) “Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness. And let steadfastness have its full effect, that you may be perfect and complete, lacking in nothing.” —(James 1:2-4)

Deliberately choose harder moral decisions instead of easier ones. Practice functioning when emotions are disrupted rather than seeking comfort. Find ways to serve others during your own hardship instead of just receiving care. When my father died, I chose to comfort others at the funeral instead of just grieving—it built spiritual muscle I didn’t know I needed. This is how God designed character formation: through trials that build steadfastness, not through comfort that builds dependence.

Week 25+: Strategic Development (Leadership Training) “Therefore, since we are surrounded by so great a cloud of witnesses, let us also lay aside every weight, and sin which clings so closely, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith.” —(Hebrews 12:1-2)

Start thinking long-term about kingdom advancement, not just personal spiritual growth. Develop ability to provide stability for others during crisis rather than needing constant support. Learn to discern spiritual opposition and respond strategically rather than emotionally. This is where you become the spiritual adult your family, church, and community desperately need—someone who can run the race with endurance while helping others do the same.
