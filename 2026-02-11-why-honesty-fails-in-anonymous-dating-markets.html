How information asymmetry turns honest signaling into a vulnerability, and what actually solves it

Shammah Chancellor

2026-02-11

If you’ve spent any time on dating apps, you’ve probably noticed that being honest about what you want doesn’t work.

State your preferences clearly—“looking for marriage and long-term commitment”—and you don’t get partners who want the same thing. You get people who say they want the same thing, extract value for weeks or months, then reveal they never intended what they claimed.

This isn’t bad luck. It’s structural.

In anonymous markets with no reputation costs, honesty doesn’t filter for honest partners. It makes you exploitable. And the more honest you are, the easier you are to exploit.

The Information Asymmetry Problem

Dating is fundamentally an information problem. You need to know:

  • Does this person actually want what they say they want?
  • Is their financial situation what they claim?
  • Are they capable of the commitment they’re signaling?
  • Will they follow through on stated intentions?
  • Is the person they’re presenting the person they actually are?

In a functioning market, you’d have ways to verify these things. In anonymous dating markets, you don’t.

What you have instead: - A profile they wrote - Photos they chose - Responses they crafted - Stories they tell - Behaviors they perform

What you don’t have: - Independent verification of any of it - Their actual relationship history - What previous partners would say - Their reputation in communities they’re part of - Whether they present differently in other contexts

This creates massive information asymmetry. They know the truth about themselves. You know only what they choose to show you. And in anonymous markets, there’s no mechanism to close that gap.

Why Honest Signaling Backfires

Here’s what happens when you’re honest about what you want on dating apps:

You state clearly: “I’m looking for someone who wants marriage, shares financial transparency before serious commitment, wants children, values direct communication, and is building a stable career.”

What this does: 1. Filters out almost nobody (everyone can claim these things) 2. Creates a template for deception (shows exactly what to say to get access to you) 3. Makes you a target for people seeking resource extraction 4. Signals that you’re serious/successful (makes you more valuable to exploit)

The people who actually match your preferences AND are honest will respond. But so will people who match none of your preferences and are dishonest. And you can’t tell the difference until you’ve invested significant time and resources.

The honest people state their real preferences, match with you if compatible, invest genuinely.

The dishonest people mirror your stated preferences perfectly, gain access, extract value (time, money, emotional investment, sexual access, social proof), then exit when costs catch up or better opportunities appear.

In anonymous markets, you can’t distinguish between these groups until after you’ve incurred costs.

The Empirical Evidence: Dual Identity Exploitation

I discovered this pattern the hard way through multiple relationships where women misrepresented their situations:

  • Woman leaving bridal catalogs around, taking me to look at rings at Tiffany’s and Cartier, but refusing to discuss finances before marriage (my non-negotiable). Turns out: unemployed, no savings, parents paying her rent. The “traditional” preference for me paying for everything wasn’t cultural—it was economic necessity she was hiding.

  • Multiple women presenting as wanting long-term relationships while actually seeking financial transactions.

After the third or fourth time this pattern emerged, I did something that proved the information asymmetry problem empirically:

I signed up for escort sites.

Not to use them—to cross-reference. To see if there was overlap between the women on dating apps and the women on platforms where the transaction is explicit.

What I found:

Significant overlap. Same women on both platforms. But very different self-presentation:

Dating app profile: - “Looking for something real” - “Tired of games and players” - “Want a genuine connection” - “Interested in marriage and family” - Professional photos, relationship-focused bio

Escort site profile: - Rates listed clearly - Services specified - Availability posted - Transactional framing - Same person, same photos, completely different story

Same woman. Two identities. One honest about the transaction (escort site). One dishonest to gain access that honesty wouldn’t provide (dating app).

This isn’t a moral judgment—it’s rational behavior in anonymous markets. If you can: - Present as wanting a relationship on dating apps - Extract resources (dinners, gifts, rent money, travel, “loans”) - Exit when discovered or demands escalate - Face zero reputation consequences - Repeat with new targets on the same platform

…then why wouldn’t you? The incentives reward the behavior. The only cost is getting caught by one person—who you then block and move on.

Why This Isn’t Just “Gold Digging”

The traditional “gold digger” pattern was different:

Traditional gold digger: Woman actually wants to marry wealthy man. She’s honest about wanting marriage (though dishonest about loving him). The transaction is explicit: marriage for financial security. Both parties understand the deal, even if they don’t state it openly.

Anonymous market exploitation: No intent to marry. No intent for relationship. Pure resource extraction with relationship framing as access mechanism. The “relationship seeker” presentation is strategic misrepresentation to gain access that honest presentation (escort/sugar baby/transactional) wouldn’t provide.

The difference: traditional gold diggers delivered the stated good (marriage). Anonymous market exploiters never intended to deliver anything except continued access as long as extraction was possible.

The Adverse Selection Mechanism

This is classic adverse selection:

In markets with information asymmetry, the people willing to transact are disproportionately those with hidden negative qualities. The good-quality participants exit because they get exploited. What remains is increasingly lower quality.

Applied to dating markets:

  1. Honest people state preferences clearly → become targets for strategic misrepresentation
  2. Dishonest people mirror stated preferences → gain access they wouldn’t otherwise have
  3. Honest people get burned repeatedly → become more guarded or exit the market
  4. Dishonest people remain in the pool → because exploitation keeps working
  5. Dating pool quality degrades → increasingly consists of people who lie/exploit

The honest people either: - Exit the market entirely (stop dating) - Become dishonest themselves (adopt exploitative strategies) - Become extremely guarded (treat everyone as suspect, which prevents genuine connection)

None of these outcomes produce functional relationships.

Why Anonymous Markets Can’t Solve This

Dating apps can’t fix information asymmetry because the fundamental architecture prevents it:

No reputation system that works: - Can’t see someone’s actual relationship history - Can’t talk to their ex-partners - Can’t verify claims about their life/situation - Reviews would be gamed immediately

No verification mechanisms: - Photos can be old/edited/fake - Bios can claim anything - Employment/education can be fabricated - Even “verified” profiles just confirm you’re a real person, not that you’re honest

No consequences for misrepresentation: - Get caught lying to one person? Block them. - Reported for scamming? Make new profile. - Burned enough people they warn others? Change photos/bio. - Platform bans you? Different platform, different identity.

The architecture requires anonymity (privacy concerns, safety issues) but anonymity enables exploitation. There’s no way around this trade-off within the app framework.

What Actually Works: Community-Based Vetting

The solution isn’t better apps. It’s abandoning anonymous markets entirely for relationship formation.

Community-based matching solves information asymmetry through mechanisms anonymous markets can’t replicate:

1. Reputation Over Time

In communities where people interact repeatedly over months/years, your actual character becomes known. You can’t maintain dual identities when the community sees you consistently in multiple contexts.

The woman running dual dating app/escort site profiles? In a tight-knit community of 30-100 people, that doesn’t work. Your actual situation, behavior patterns, and treatment of people become visible through repeated interaction.

2. Distributed Knowledge

Community vetting isn’t one person’s judgment. It’s distributed knowledge from multiple observers over time:

  • How do they treat people when they’re not trying to impress?
  • How do they handle conflict?
  • How do they respond to stress?
  • Do they follow through on commitments?
  • What do their ex-partners say?
  • How do they treat service workers, elders, children?

This information emerges naturally through community interaction. You don’t need to ask—you observe over time, and others share their observations.

3. Reputation Costs for Bad Behavior

In anonymous markets, the cost of burning one person is zero (block and move on).

In communities, the cost of burning one person is your reputation with everyone. Word spreads. Your behavior toward one person signals your character to all observers. Future opportunities depend on maintaining trustworthiness.

This creates strong incentives against exploitation. The short-term gain from deceiving one person isn’t worth the long-term cost to your reputation.

4. Can’t Fake Long-Term Patterns

You can fake interest for a few dates. You can’t fake character for years of community interaction.

The person who presents as wanting marriage on dating apps but is actually seeking resource extraction? In a community, their actual pattern becomes visible: - Do they maintain long-term commitments? - How do they handle responsibilities? - What’s their actual financial situation? - Do their stated intentions match their behavior?

Short-term deception is possible. Long-term deception in communities where people know you across multiple contexts is nearly impossible.

5. Network Validation

In anonymous markets, you evaluate someone based on what they tell you and how they act with you.

In communities, you evaluate someone based on: - What they tell you - How they act with you - What mutual friends observe - What their reputation is - How they’re known over time - Whether their stated history matches what others saw

The woman who claimed to be “traditional” while hiding unemployment? In a community setting, her actual financial situation would be known. The bridal catalog hints instead of direct communication would have been observed by others and discussed. The refusal to discuss finances before marriage would raise red flags that friends would mention.

Why Modern People Resist This

“But I don’t want my community involved in my dating life.”

This is the individualism that enables the exploitation.

You want privacy. You want autonomy. You want to make decisions without community input.

What you get instead: - Anonymous markets full of people who can lie with zero consequences - Information asymmetry where you can’t verify anything - Repeated exploitation by people who present false identities - No mechanism to distinguish honest from dishonest signals - Dating pool that degrades as honest people exit

You’re choosing privacy over functionality. You’re choosing autonomy over coordination. You’re choosing individual decision-making over distributed knowledge.

And the result is you get exploited, repeatedly, by people who understand the game better than you do.

The Equilibrium: Everyone Lies

Here’s where anonymous dating markets inevitably end:

Honest signaling is punished: - State real preferences → become target for exploitation - Be transparent about situation → makes you less appealing than people who lie - Communicate directly about needs → shows vulnerability that gets weaponized

Dishonest signaling is rewarded: - Mirror their preferences → gain access - Hide your actual situation → maintain appeal - Say what they want to hear → extract value longer

Result: The equilibrium becomes everyone lying.

The honest people adapt or exit. The dishonest people thrive. The market becomes increasingly full of people who’ve learned that honesty doesn’t work.

And then everyone complains that “there are no good people left in the dating pool.”

The good people are there. They just learned to be dishonest because honesty got them destroyed.

The Alternative

If you’re serious about finding a functional long-term relationship, the path isn’t better profiles or better apps.

It’s getting out of anonymous markets entirely.

Join communities where: - People know each other over months/years - Reputation matters because you’ll see these people repeatedly - Distributed knowledge about character is available - Bad behavior has social consequences - You can observe someone across multiple contexts before romantic involvement

This means: - Church communities (small enough to actually know people, not megachurches) - Hobby communities with regular interaction - Mutual aid networks - Tight-knit professional communities - Volunteer organizations - Anything where people interact repeatedly in multiple contexts

The goal isn’t to “find dates” in these communities. The goal is to be part of communities where: 1. Your character is observed over time 2. Others’ character is observed over time 3. When romantic interest develops, there’s already distributed knowledge about both people 4. The community can provide input, feedback, and accountability 5. Deception is costly because reputation affects all future interactions

The Uncomfortable Truth

Anonymous dating markets can’t be fixed. The architecture that enables privacy and safety also enables exploitation. Information asymmetry is structural, not solvable with better matching algorithms.

You have two choices:

Option 1: Stay in anonymous markets. Accept that honest signaling will make you exploitable. Accept that you can’t distinguish honest from dishonest participants until after costs are incurred. Accept that the market selects for people who lie effectively. Adapt by becoming more guarded, more strategic, more willing to misrepresent—or exit entirely.

Option 2: Abandon anonymous markets for community-based vetting. Accept that this requires giving up privacy, autonomy, and individual decision-making. Accept that the community will be involved in your dating life. Accept that your character will be evaluated by others over time, and their character will be evaluated too.

There’s no third option that gives you both privacy and reliable information. You can have anonymous markets with information asymmetry, or community-based vetting with reputation costs. You can’t have both.

The question is which coordination failure you’re willing to tolerate: the exploitation that comes with anonymity, or the lack of privacy that comes with community vetting.

Modern dating is failing because we’re trying to maintain individual autonomy while solving a collective information problem. It doesn’t work. It can’t work. The architecture prevents it.

Community-based vetting works because it sacrifices individual privacy for collective information. And until people are willing to make that trade-off, anonymous markets will continue selecting for dishonesty.

The swine will keep drowning.

Subscribe for analysis that doesn’t pretend the system works. Anonymous markets reward deception. Community vetting solves it. The choice is yours, but pretending there’s a third option doesn’t make it exist.
