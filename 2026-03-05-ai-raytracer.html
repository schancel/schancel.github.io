AI Is Rendering for Text

2026-03-05 · Shammah Chancellor
You have to let it render.

The people getting the worst results from AI coding tools are also the most confident they understand how to use them. They type a prompt, read the output, decide it’s wrong, type another prompt. Fast, impatient, frustrated. They think prompting is like ordering from a menu. It isn’t. It’s rendering.

Rendering is always the same process: apply energy over time and what’s latent becomes clarified. When you fry bacon, you’re rendering the fat; heat drives out what was always in the meat and leaves behind something clarified. When a path tracer resolves a 3D scene, each pass drives out noise and leaves behind light as it actually falls. You’re not creating something from nothing. You’re clarifying something that exists in potential.

AI is rendering for text.

You Have to Hold the Vision

The first mistake: expecting the first output to be the final output. They type a prompt, get something rough, and conclude the tool doesn’t work. That’s like stopping a raytracer after the first ray and declaring ray tracing overrated.

The picture sharpens as you iterate. First pass is rough; you’re blocking out shapes, getting the topology right. Each prompt refines it. You’re not correcting mistakes so much as focusing the lens. By the end you’re looking at exactly what you had in your head, but you couldn’t have described it upfront. You had to see what wasn’t right to know what right looked like.

This is why AI rewards experienced programmers disproportionately. You can’t direct a rendering engine if you don’t know what the final scene should look like. Junior developers ask for code and get code-shaped objects: technically correct, architecturally wrong. Senior developers get what they pictured, because they can feel when the render is off and keep refining until it isn’t.

The craft shifts from writing code to knowing what to ask for. Different skill. Same requirement: taste.

The objection: can’t anyone just iterate more and get there? No. You can’t iterate toward a destination you can’t see. A path tracer with no scene to render just produces static. The limitation isn’t effort. It’s vision.

The Bacon Rule

The second mistake: impatience.

Frying bacon takes as long as it takes. You can turn the heat up and burn it before the fat renders. You can flip it too early and lose the crispness. You can leave it too long; overcooking is its own failure mode. Or you can tend it, low and slow, one side at a time, and get exactly what you wanted.

AI coding is the same. Every complex feature has a natural resolution time. You can’t compress it by throwing more prompts at it before the previous ones have settled. The developers who get the best results read the output carefully, understand what it did and didn’t do, and make one precise correction at a time. They’re tending the pan.

The ones who get garbage are typing furiously, overriding half-finished outputs, flipping the bacon before the fat has rendered.

A McKinsey study on AI-assisted developer productivity (https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/unleashing-developer-productivity-with-generative-ai) found the biggest variance wasn’t between tools but between how developers used them; specifically, whether they read outputs carefully before responding. Experience can work against you here. The more you know, the harder it is to stop interrupting the render.

Patience isn’t passivity. Tending the pan is active. Focused attention, not frantic intervention.

What It’s Actually Like

When it really works, AI coding feels like thinking out loud to someone who finally understands you. Metaphors just appear. Connections you hadn’t made. It helps you paint. You describe the shape of a problem and a solution surfaces. Not the final solution; a first approximation. You look at it, see what’s slightly wrong, describe the correction, and it sharpens. A few passes later you have something you’d have been proud to write by hand, produced in a fraction of the time.

Every output is a render pass. You’re not evaluating whether it’s done; you’re evaluating whether it’s getting closer.

Programmers who treat AI as a vending machine are using a raytracer to take a screenshot. They’re getting something, but they’re not getting the scene they could have.

The rendering metaphor isn’t just an analogy; there’s a sense in which it’s literally true. A language model is a vast geometric space where every word, concept, and code pattern exists as a point. I’m the raycaster. My intent is the beam; the model is the geometry it passes through. Each prompt sets a direction, and the output is wherever my ray lands: a probable surface close to what I meant. Each iteration refines the angle.

Nobody says this clearly enough. AI doesn’t make bad programmers good. It makes good programmers faster. GitHub’s research found 55% faster task completion with Copilot (https://github.blog/news-insights/research/research-quantifying-github-copilots-impact-on-developer-productivity-and-happiness/) — but that measures speed on isolated tasks, not architectural quality over months. Stack Overflow’s 2023 developer survey (https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2023/) found AI tool adoption was highest among senior developers; that contradicts the narrative that AI is a junior-dev shortcut. The people getting the most out of it are the ones who needed it least. That’s not a coincidence; that’s amplification.

I’ve built systems this way that I couldn’t have built alone in the same timeframe. Not because the AI was smarter than me; it wasn’t. But because the render time, the translation from idea to running code, collapsed to almost nothing. Every hour I used to spend on implementation I now spend on design. The output is better because the thinking got more of my time.

Not that AI writes code. It removes everything that isn’t thinking, and lets you find out how good your thinking actually is.

Pull It at the Right Moment

One more thing the bacon teaches you: know when to stop.

Over-rendered bacon is just as bad as under-rendered. Over-iterated code is the same. There’s a point where the output is good, genuinely good, and continuing to refine it makes it worse. You start adding complexity where simplicity served. The fat is gone and now you’re burning meat.

Good programmers know that moment. They’ve burned enough bacon to recognize it. AI makes it easier to overshoot because iteration is so cheap; one more prompt costs nothing, so why not? But the cost is invisible and cumulative. The code gets more complex, harder to maintain, further from elegant. Knowing when to stop is as much a skill as knowing how to start.

Pull it at the right moment. Know when the render is done.

You still have to hold the vision. You still have to tend the bacon.

But if you can do that, if you have the taste and the patience to let things resolve, you are working with a tool that has no precedent. The gap between what you can imagine and what you can build has never been smaller. That’s not an accident; that’s rendering.
