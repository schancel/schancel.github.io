The Information-Processing Nature of Narrative

Narratives are information processing systems. They provide causal frameworks for understanding why things happen, moral frameworks for evaluating whether things should happen, predictive frameworks for anticipating what will happen, and identity frameworks for understanding who you are in the story.

When narrative coherence collapses, populations lose the tools necessary for reality processing. This is inevitable and strategically exploitable.

The Hebrew Understanding of Narrative

Hebrews 11 presents history as coherent narrative where actions have consequences across time, faith produces observable outcomes, individual choices affect collective story, and divine purpose unifies apparently disconnected events.

Now faith is the assurance of things hoped for, the conviction of things not seen. —(Hebrews 11:1)

Faith functions as narrative coherence—the framework that connects present actions to future outcomes even when the connection isn’t immediately visible.

What Narrative Fragmentation Looks Like

Here’s what it looks like in your daily life:

Family dinner conversation about inflation: - Your dad: “It’s all the government spending—basic economics!” - Your sister: “No, it’s corporate greed—they’re price-gouging during a crisis!” - Your cousin: “You’re both wrong—it’s a planned economic reset to control us!”

Same data, same family, three completely incompatible explanations. No shared criteria for determining which is true.

Church discussion about cultural issues: - Member 1: “We need to stand for biblical values against cultural decline!” - Member 2: “We need to show more love and acceptance to marginalized people!” - Member 3: “We need to focus on the gospel, not politics!”

Same Scripture, same faith community, but everyone’s operating from different foundational assumptions about what Christianity requires.

The fragmentation is so complete that even people who love each other can’t find common ground for basic conversation. It’s not that people are stupid—it’s that they’re processing reality through completely different systems.

The Strategic Purpose of Fragmentation

Strategic fragmentation serves five objectives:

  1. Paralyze collective action - Populations cannot coordinate around shared goals without shared reality
  2. Prevent pattern recognition - Fragmented narratives eliminate ability to perceive strategic patterns
  3. Create authority dependency - Reality-processing failure creates desperate dependence on “expert” interpretation
  4. Generate emotional chaos - Narrative fragmentation predictably produces anxiety, depression, and rage
  5. Prevent stable community formation - Shared narrative is the requirement for social cohesion

The goal: Atomize human society until collective resistance becomes impossible.

The Destruction of Natural Coordination Points

Think about “natural coordination points”—places everyone would naturally choose to meet if they couldn’t communicate specifics.

Twenty years ago, if you needed to meet someone in New York City at noon but couldn’t communicate the location, most people would instinctively choose Times Square or Grand Central Station. These worked because everyone shared the same cultural knowledge about what was “obviously” the right choice.

Here’s what’s terrifying: Those obvious meeting points don’t work anymore.

Try this experiment with people from different political tribes, different generations, different media diets. Ask where they’d meet someone in their city without being able to communicate. You’ll get completely different answers because they no longer share the cultural framework that makes anything “obvious.”

My friend Sarah experienced this firsthand. She tried to organize a neighborhood response to a local issue. People couldn’t even agree on what the problem was, let alone where to meet or what to do about it. Everyone was getting their information from different sources that painted completely different pictures of reality.

The fragmentation is so complete that basic community coordination has become impossible. People can’t work together because they can’t even agree on what “working together” means.

Narrative fragmentation operates as the perfect coordination destroyer—it eliminates not only explicit coordination but the implicit coordination that emerges through culturally shared points. This creates logical impossibility of collective action.

Your Sanity Recovery Strategy

Week 1: Anchor to Unchanging Reality Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever. —(Hebrews 13:8)

Practice: Each morning, identify three unchanging truths that no amount of media manipulation can alter: God’s character, moral reality, logical principles. When information chaos overwhelms you, return to these anchors.

I do this daily—it’s the only thing that keeps me sane. While narratives shift and authorities contradict each other, I know that kindness is still better than cruelty, truth is still better than lies, and love is still better than hate. These realities don’t depend on expert opinion or political trends.

Week 2: Think From First Principles Practice: Before accepting any narrative, trace it back to foundational assumptions. Ask: “What would have to be true for this to make sense? Do I actually believe those assumptions?” My friend Tom started doing this and realized 90% of his anxiety came from accepting narratives built on assumptions he didn’t actually hold.

Week 3: Trust Your Own Eyes But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil. —(Hebrews 5:14)

Practice: Distinguish between what you actually observe and what media tells you to believe about your observations. When I started doing this, I realized that most of my “information” about the world came from screens, not actual experience.

Week 4: Build Reality-Based Community Not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another, and all the more as you see the Day drawing near. —(Hebrews 10:25)

Practice: Cultivate relationships with people you can observe directly. How do they actually live? What are their actual character patterns? Shared narrative emerges from shared experience, not shared media consumption.

Week 5: Judge by Results, Not Rhetoric By their fruits you will know them. Do people pick grapes from thornbushes, or figs from thistles? —(Matthew 7:16)

Practice: Evaluate all narratives by measurable outcomes. Do they predict accurately? Do they produce observable flourishing? Do they help people live better lives? This single practice eliminates 95% of manufactured narratives because they don’t actually work.
