Why Multimillionaires Telling Average Men to Be Providers Is a Scam

Shammah Chancellor

2026-02-10

Scott Galloway—NYU professor, multimillionaire entrepreneur, podcast host—has made a career telling young men to “man up.” Embrace traditional masculinity. Become providers. Take responsibility. Stop playing video games and start building wealth to attract women and support families.

His audience eats it up. Struggling young men desperate for direction, for someone to tell them what went wrong and how to fix it. The advice sounds tough, masculine, no-nonsense. It sounds like what their grandfathers would have said.

There’s just one problem: Scott Galloway is worth hundreds of millions of dollars. His advice only works if you’re Scott Galloway.

The Hypocrisy Is Structural, Not Personal

I’m not attacking Galloway’s character. Maybe he genuinely wants to help. Maybe he believes his own advice. The problem isn’t his intentions; it’s that his success as a husband and father depends entirely on resources that his audience will never have.

Without his hundreds of millions of dollars, his partner would have left him. He’d have lost his kids in family court. He’d be another divorced man paying child support while seeing his children every other weekend. The stable family he presents as the fruit of “manning up” exists because he has elite-level wealth, not because the traditional provider model works for average men.

Yet he tells average men—men who will never make millions, who don’t have his network or opportunities or starting capital—to embrace the exact role that fails catastrophically for most men who attempt it.

This isn’t mentorship. It’s selling broken maps to people who are lost.

Why Men Are Dropping Out

Men now make up only 40% of college students. Male workforce participation has been declining for decades. Marriage rates are collapsing, birth rates cratering. Men are checking out of education, careers, relationships, and family formation at unprecedented rates.

The advisory class—Galloway and his peers—diagnoses this as a failure of masculinity. Men have become weak, lazy, addicted to screens and pornography. They need to man up, take responsibility, embrace traditional provider roles.

But what if men are responding rationally to incentives?

Consider what “manning up” as a provider actually means for an average man in 2026:

You spend your twenties building career capital. You delay gratification, work long hours, accumulate skills and credentials. You finally reach a point where you’re earning decent money—not Galloway money, but solid middle-class income. You’re ready to be a provider.

You meet a woman. She’s also college-educated, also earning decent money. You date, you get serious, you propose. The advice says this is success—you manned up, you became a provider, you’re doing it right.

Then you get married.

Your income is now joint property. Your assets are joint property. Your future earnings are joint property. But the marriage itself? That’s entirely at her discretion. No-fault divorce means she can leave anytime, for any reason or no reason at all.

When she does—and divorce is initiated by women in 69% of cases—here’s what happens:

Family courts award her primary custody of the children. You become a weekend dad, seeing your kids every other weekend if you’re lucky. You pay child support based on your income. You pay alimony if she’s not earning as much. You lose half your assets. You might lose the house.

Meanwhile, she receives your financial support while having complete freedom to pursue other relationships. Your children watch their mother’s new boyfriend move into what used to be your home. You continue paying to support the family unit you’ve been removed from.

This is the system Scott Galloway tells men to enthusiastically embrace.

The Coordination Structures That Made Provider Roles Work

Traditional male provider roles weren’t based on men’s moral superiority or women’s subordination. They were coordination mechanisms that solved real problems.

Historical system:

When your grandfather was a provider, he lived in a world with functional coordination structures: - Commitment enforcement: Community pressure and social consequences made leaving marriages difficult for both parties; no-fault divorce didn’t exist - Custody presumptions: Fathers retained custody rights; mothers couldn’t unilaterally remove children - Economic integration: Wife’s household labor and husband’s income were genuinely interdependent, not just parallel incomes - Community support: Extended family and local community helped enforce norms and support struggling marriages - Legal protections: Men weren’t assumed to be domestic abusers; family courts didn’t systematically favor mothers

In this context, being a provider made sense. Your investment in the relationship was protected. Your role as father was secure. Your economic contribution created genuine partnership, not just exposure to financial extraction.

Modern system:

Now consider what changed: - No commitment enforcement: Either party can exit anytime, for any reason; social consequences are minimal - Presumptive maternal custody: Family courts overwhelmingly favor mothers; fathers fight uphill battles for equal parenting - Economic extraction: Your income becomes her divorce settlement; you pay to support children you don’t get to parent - Atomization: No community to support marriages or enforce norms; couples are isolated units - Legal bias: Domestic violence accusations (true or false) used strategically in custody battles; men presumed guilty

Telling men to “man up” and be providers in this system is like telling them to enthusiastically sign contracts where the other party can terminate anytime while keeping all your assets. It’s not masculinity; it’s financial suicide.

What Works for Elite Men Doesn’t Work for Average Men

Here’s why Scott Gallloway’s marriage works while average-income provider marriages fail:

Resource asymmetry: When you’re worth hundreds of millions, you can afford the best divorce lawyers, fight for custody, structure prenuptial agreements that actually protect assets. Average men get court-appointed representation or go into debt hiring mediocre lawyers.

Hypergamous selection stability: Galloway’s wealth places him at the extreme top of male economic hierarchy. His partner can’t “trade up” easily because there aren’t many men wealthier and higher-status. Average men face constant hypergamous pressure—their partners can always find men with more resources, more status, better options.

Social proof and status: Galloway’s public success, professional reputation, and social networks create stability through status lock-in. Average men have no status protection; they’re replaceable.

Institutional capture: Wealthy men can navigate or avoid systems that destroy average men. They have accountants, lawyers, financial structures that protect assets. They have social capital to influence outcomes.

The traditional provider role works fine if you’re in the top 1% of male economic hierarchy. It fails catastrophically for everyone else. Yet Galloway sells the model to men who will never have his advantages, as if the problem is their lack of masculinity rather than the absence of coordination structures that made the role functional.

The Grift

Here’s what makes this particularly insidious: Scott Galloway profits from male dysfunction.

His podcasts, his books, his speaking fees—all of it depends on a steady stream of struggling young men looking for answers. He’s not solving their problems; he’s monetizing their confusion. Every man who buys his advice, listens to his podcast, attends his talks—that’s money flowing from men who are failing in the system to a man who succeeded despite the system.

If Galloway actually wanted to help, he’d be advocating for: - Legal reform: Presumptive equal custody, elimination of no-fault divorce advantages, protection against false accusations - Economic support: Policies that make average-income men viable providers, not just elite men - Coordination repair: Community structures that enforce commitment and support marriages - Honest accounting: Acknowledge that traditional roles require traditional coordination structures

Instead, he sells personal responsibility narratives that blame men for structural failures. This serves two purposes:

First, it protects the status quo. If the problem is men’s character rather than broken institutions, nothing needs to change systemically. The elite can keep capturing resources while telling struggling men they just need to work harder.

Second, it’s profitable. Personal responsibility narratives sell. Moral exhortation is cheap. Structural diagnosis is complicated and doesn’t fit podcast formats.

Stop Enriching People Who Profit From Your Dysfunction

If you’re a young man listening to Scott Galloway or similar influencers—stop. You’re making a rich man richer while getting advice that doesn’t work for your actual situation.

Galloway’s success as a provider and father depends on resources you will never have. His marriage stability depends on legal and social advantages you cannot access. His children’s presence in his life depends on wealth-mediated custody outcomes that average men don’t get.

You are not Scott Galloway. His advice is not for you. It’s for him—it makes him money, builds his brand, and reinforces the system that works for elite men while destroying average men.

What Men Actually Need

Men dropping out of college, workforce, and relationships aren’t failing. They’re responding rationally to incentives. When the provider role offers massive downside risk (financial destruction, loss of children, legal persecution) with minimal upside (unstable relationships, no custody protection, no commitment enforcement), checking out is the rational choice.

What would actually help:

Coordination repair, not moral exhortation: You can’t revive traditional provider roles without reviving the coordination structures that made them functional. This means: - Community enforcement of commitment (not just exit freedom) - Legal systems that protect providers rather than punish them - Custody presumptions that don’t systematically favor mothers - Economic pathways that make average-income provision viable

Honest diagnosis: Stop blaming men’s character for structural failures. The problem isn’t that men are weak; it’s that the system is broken.

Alternative models: If traditional roles don’t work without traditional coordination, what does? Maybe men need to build parallel structures—communities that enforce norms, economic cooperation that bypasses broken institutions, alternative family models that protect against legal extraction.

Stop funding the grift: Don’t buy advice from multimillionaires telling you to embrace roles that only work with their resources. Their success doesn’t validate the model; it proves the model only works for elite men.

The Advice Class Is Parasitic

Scott Galloway and similar influencers occupy a parasitic position. They profit from diagnosing problems they don’t solve, offering advice that doesn’t work, selling narratives that reinforce the dysfunction they’re monetizing.

They’re not helping. They’re extracting value from collapse.

Men struggling with broken coordination systems don’t need tough-love podcasts from millionaires. They need structural repair—legal reform, economic support, community coordination, honest accounting of what actually makes provider roles functional versus what makes them exploitative.

Until that repair happens, “manning up” is just another way of saying “accept your financial destruction and loss of your children while making me wealthy.”

That’s not mentorship. That’s a scam.
