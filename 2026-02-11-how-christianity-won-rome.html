The coordination mechanisms that survived civilizational collapse—and why the internet changes everything

Shammah Chancellor

2026-02-11

Rome didn’t fall to Christianity because Christians had better arguments. It fell because Christians had better coordination when everything else collapsed.

This matters because we’re living through similar civilizational breakdown. The question isn’t whether Christianity will emerge as a coordination solution again—it’s whether Christians are smart enough to rebuild deliberately instead of stumbling into it through centuries of collapse.

And this time, the internet changes the timeline dramatically.

The Standard Story Is Wrong

The textbook narrative goes like this: Christianity spread through missionary work, converts believed the gospel, Constantine converted, Christianity became the state religion. Theology won hearts and minds.

This is mostly false.

Christianity grew from approximately 1,000 people (30 AD) to 6 million (300 AD) before Constantine’s conversion. That’s 40% growth per decade for 270 years. Sociologist Rodney Stark showed this growth rate is perfectly consistent with network effects and functional superiority during crisis, not mass conversions from persuasive preaching.

The actual story: Christianity built coordination mechanisms that worked when Roman systems failed. When crisis came, those mechanisms produced measurable survival advantages. Survivors converted. Networks grew. The cycle repeated.

This wasn’t primarily about theology. It was about function.

What Early Christians Actually Did

The Archaeological and Historical Record

We have extensive non-biblical evidence for what early Christians actually practiced:

Pliny the Younger (112 AD) - Roman governor writing to Emperor Trajan: > “They were accustomed to meet on a fixed day before dawn and sing responsively a hymn to Christ as to a god, and to bind themselves by oath, not to some crime, but not to commit fraud, theft, or adultery, not to falsify their trust… After this it was their custom to depart and to assemble again to partake of food—but ordinary and innocent food.”

This isn’t Christian propaganda. This is a hostile witness documenting that Christians: - Met regularly in organized groups - Made binding commitments to behavioral standards - Shared communal meals - Had systematic coordination across cities

Lucian of Samosata (165 AD) - Greek satirist mocking Christians: > “Their first lawgiver persuaded them that they are all brothers… Therefore they despise all material goods alike and hold them in common… If any charlatan or trickster comes among them, he quickly acquires wealth by imposing upon simple people.”

Hostile source. But confirms: - Christians practiced economic pooling - Maintained networks supporting imprisoned members - Provided material support across communities - Had systematic mutual aid (which Lucian thinks makes them gullible)

Celsus (178 AD) - Pagan philosopher attacking Christianity: > “Christians show their love of truth by accepting only the poor, the vulgar, and the stupid… wool-workers, cobblers, laundry-workers, and the most illiterate and rustic yokels.”

He’s complaining that Christianity recruited lower classes by providing economic support and community. Inadvertent confirmation that coordination mechanisms worked for those Roman systems excluded.

Tertullian (197 AD) - Christian apologist describing actual practices: > “On the monthly collection day… each puts in a small amount—but only if he wishes and only if he is able. There is no compulsion… These gifts are spent on supporting and burying poor people, to supply the wants of boys and girls destitute of means and parents, and of old persons confined to the house; for shipwrecked sailors, and for any in the mines, on islands, or in prisons.”

This describes systematic redistribution infrastructure: voluntary contributions, formal collection days, organized distribution to widows, orphans, elderly, prisoners, disaster victims.

This isn’t charity. This is coordinated mutual aid with bureaucratic organization.

The Specific Mechanisms

House Churches (30-75 people):

Archaeological evidence from Dura-Europos (Syria, 240 AD) shows house churches accommodated 50-75 people. Literary sources confirm similar scale. This size matters:

  • Small enough: Everyone knows everyone’s character over years. Reputation has teeth. You can’t fake it.
  • Large enough: Provides marriage pool, economic support, distributed knowledge for vetting
  • Replicable: When group hits capacity, split and plant new church. Network growth through division.

This scale solves the information asymmetry problem. You’re not trusting a stranger from a dating app. You’re trusting someone the community has observed for years.

Systematic Widow Support (1 Timothy 5:3-16):

Churches maintained formal rolls of widows 60+ with no family support. Requirements: - Age 60+ (younger widows expected to remarry) - “Wife of one husband” (monogamous marriage history) - Good reputation for hospitality and service - In exchange: community support, role in teaching younger women

This isn’t ad-hoc charity. This is structured mutual aid with eligibility requirements and reciprocal obligations. The specificity reveals systematic coordination.

Agape Meals (Love Feasts):

Weekly communal meals where wealthy members brought food, poor members ate freely. Not just fellowship—wealth transfer through hospitality.

When Paul criticizes Corinthian church (1 Corinthians 11:20-22), he’s angry that wealthy Christians ate separately while poor went hungry. His rebuke reveals how seriously early Christians took economic coordination. Failure to share meals wasn’t just rude—it violated the core coordination mechanism.

Travel Networks:

Christians traveling for trade or mission carried letters of recommendation (Romans 16:1-2). Receiving churches provided housing, resources, connections.

This created distributed trust infrastructure: You couldn’t reinvent yourself in the next town. The network preceded you. Your reputation traveled through letters. Community vetting extended across cities.

Financial Coordination:

Cyprian of Carthage (250s AD) documented raising 100,000 sesterces to ransom Christians captured by barbarians. Paul organized systematic collections from Greek churches to support Jerusalem church during famine (2 Corinthians 8-9).

This wasn’t local charity. This was network-wide resource coordination across hundreds of miles.

Why These Mechanisms Worked

These practices solved real coordination problems the Roman system couldn’t:

Problem: Economic precarity, no safety net → Solution: Mutual aid pools, systematic widow support Problem: Information asymmetry in marriage markets → Solution: Community vetting over years, distributed knowledge Problem: Trust breakdown across distance → Solution: Letter networks, reputation systems Problem: Plague and crisis overwhelming individual families → Solution: Coordinated care infrastructure

The theology provided motivation and consistency. But the survival advantage came from solving functional coordination problems.

The Crisis That Proved the System

Antonine Plague (165-180 AD)

The first major test. Possibly smallpox or measles. Killed up to one-third of the empire’s population.

Pagan Response: Galen (greatest Roman physician) fled Rome for his country estate. Wealthy families abandoned sick family members, threw bodies into streets to avoid contagion. Cities experienced mass death with minimal organized response.

Christian Response: Bishop Dionysius of Alexandria (later describing Cyprian’s Plague, but same pattern) wrote: > “Most of our brother Christians showed unbounded love and loyalty, never sparing themselves and thinking only of one another. Heedless of danger, they took charge of the sick, attending to their every need… Many, in nursing and curing others, transferred their death to themselves and died in their stead.”

Meanwhile, pagans: > “At the first onset of the disease, [they] pushed the sufferers away and fled from their dearest, throwing them into the roads before they were dead.”

The Demographic Result:

Simple nursing care—hydration, food, basic comfort during illness—dramatically improved survival rates when the standard pagan response was complete abandonment.

Christians survived at higher rates not through divine intervention but through coordinated care. And pagans who received Christian care during crisis converted in massive numbers. This wasn’t theology converting them—it was functional superiority under pressure.

Cyprian’s Plague (250-270 AD)

Named for Bishop Cyprian of Carthage who documented the Christian response. Possibly measles or hemorrhagic fever. Killed 5,000 people per day in Rome at its peak.

Cyprian’s organizing instructions (preserved in his letters): - Assigned Christians to different city districts - Systematic care for sick (Christians and pagans) - Burial teams for the dead - Resource pooling to support caregivers

This wasn’t heroic individuals. This was bureaucratic coordination at scale.

The result: Massive conversion of plague survivors who’d been cared for by Christians while pagan families abandoned them.

The Compounding Network Effect

Each plague cycle: 1. Christians coordinated care → higher survival rates 2. Survivors converted out of gratitude and recognition of functional superiority 3. Larger Christian population → more coordinated care capacity next crisis 4. Network effects compound

By 250 AD, Christianity was ~10% of the empire. By 300 AD, ~15-20%. Constantine’s conversion (312 AD) didn’t create Christian majority—it recognized the inevitable. By 400 AD, Christianity was majority religion through demographic and network advantages, not imperial decree.

What Modern Christianity Lost

Fast-forward to today. Western Christianity has ~2 billion adherents but almost none of the coordination mechanisms that made early Christianity functionally superior.

Modern megachurches: Anonymous crowds watching stage performances. No community vetting. No mutual aid making family formation viable. No reputation costs for bad behavior. Just consumer Christianity—pick your worship style, enjoy the production, go home to the same broken systems as everyone else.

Modern church “community”: Small groups that meet weekly for Bible study but provide no economic coordination, no marriage vetting, no systematic mutual aid. Emotional support but no functional coordination for the hard problems.

Culture war engagement: Posting angry takes about cultural decline, voting patterns, complaining about secularism. Zero building of alternative coordination mechanisms. Pure theater.

The practices that made Christianity survive Rome’s collapse—house churches with real accountability, mutual aid making family formation economically viable, community vetting for marriages, systematic care during crisis—modern Christianity abandoned in favor of institutional models that can’t coordinate.

Why the Internet Changes Everything

Here’s what’s different this time: Network formation costs have collapsed to near zero.

Early Christianity took 270 years to grow from 1,000 to 6 million because: - Geographic barriers limited network growth speed - Information traveled at walking/sailing speed - Finding like-minded people required physical proximity - Coordinating across cities required hand-carried letters

The internet removes all of these constraints.

Network Formation Speed

Then: To find 30-50 people in your city who share commitment to early Christian coordination mechanisms, you’d need years of relationship building, word-of-mouth, and luck.

Now: You can identify globally distributed people who understand coordination failure, want to build alternatives, and have necessary commitment levels—in weeks. Then coordinate building local clusters in multiple cities simultaneously.

Early Christianity spread through one network at walking speed. Modern Christianity could spread through parallel networks at light speed.

Distributed Knowledge Coordination

Then: Community vetting required years of in-person observation. Reputation traveled through hand-carried letters that took months.

Now: Distributed knowledge about character can be built through documented interactions, verified by multiple observers, and shared instantly. Not perfect—but faster iteration on vetting mechanisms.

The early church took generations to develop systematic vetting. Modern communities could develop, test, and refine vetting protocols in months through rapid feedback loops.

Resource Coordination

Then: Collecting 100,000 sesterces to ransom captives required traveling messengers, months of coordination, physical transport of money.

Now: Fundraising, resource allocation, and distribution can happen in days. Mutual aid pools can coordinate globally while supporting locally.

Then: Cyprian’s systematic plague response required weeks to organize.

Now: Crisis response coordination can be established in hours. Distributed teams can be organized, resources allocated, and action coordinated before institutional systems even recognize the crisis exists.

Knowledge Transfer

Then: Practices spread slowly as travelers carried knowledge between communities. Each community had to reinvent solutions.

Now: Successful coordination mechanisms can be documented, shared, tested, and iterated globally. When one community solves marriage vetting or mutual aid structure, every community can learn instantly.

Early Christianity took centuries to develop mature coordination practices. Modern Christianity could compress that development into years.

What This Means Practically

The coordination mechanisms that let early Christianity survive Rome’s collapse can be rebuilt deliberately, faster than before:

High-commitment communities can form without geographic constraints. Find 30-50 people globally who understand what’s broken and want to build alternatives, then coordinate local clustering.

Mutual aid networks can be established with modern tooling. Digital coordination makes resource pooling, systematic support, and crisis response faster and more efficient than ancient systems.

Vetting mechanisms can evolve rapidly. Test protocols, gather data, iterate based on outcomes. Early Christians took generations to develop effective vetting; modern communities could develop superior mechanisms in years through deliberate experimentation.

Knowledge sharing means no community reinvents solutions. Document what works, share across network, adopt and adapt successful patterns.

The Accelerated Timeline

Early Christianity’s growth: 270 years from 1,000 to 6 million (40% per decade).

Why that slow? Geographic and communication constraints meant network growth speed was limited by physical travel.

Modern Christianity rebuilding through internet-coordinated networks: Years, not centuries. Because:

  • Network formation: Months instead of years to find initial clusters
  • Knowledge transfer: Instant instead of generational
  • Iteration speed: Rapid testing and refinement of practices instead of trial-and-error across centuries
  • Parallel development: Multiple communities building simultaneously instead of sequential spread
  • Crisis acceleration: When next crisis hits (economic collapse, pandemic, institutional failure), coordinated communities will demonstrate functional superiority immediately instead of developing responses over generations

Early Christians built coordination mechanisms before crisis, then demonstrated superiority during plague. The demographic advantage compounded over generations.

Modern Christians could build coordination mechanisms now, demonstrate superiority during next crisis, and see network effects compound in years instead of centuries.

The Window Is Now

Here’s the critical insight from early Christianity’s success: They built the coordination mechanisms before the crisis forced them.

By the time Antonine Plague hit (165 AD), Christians already had: - House churches with systematic organization - Mutual aid infrastructure - Community care protocols - Cross-city networks

When crisis came, they didn’t scramble to build coordination—they activated existing systems.

Those who tried to build coordination during the crisis were too late. The Christians who survived were those who’d spent decades building trust infrastructure before they needed it.

We’re in the “before crisis” window right now.

Economic systems are breaking. Relationship coordination has collapsed. Institutional trust is failing. Infrastructure is decaying. But full civilizational crisis hasn’t hit yet.

This is the window to build coordination mechanisms that will prove functionally superior when crisis forces demonstration.

And unlike early Christianity, which took 270 years to build networks limited by geographic and communication constraints, the internet means we could build in years what took them centuries.

The question isn’t whether Christianity will emerge as a coordination solution again. History suggests coordination mechanisms that actually work will survive while systems that don’t will collapse.

The question is whether modern Christians are smart enough to build deliberately instead of stumbling into it through 800 years of civilizational breakdown.

Early Christians didn’t wait for divine intervention. They built the coordination mechanisms that survived Rome’s collapse through functional superiority during crisis.

The same playbook works now. But this time, network effects mean the timeline could be years instead of centuries.

The resurrection could come faster. If we’re smart enough to build it.
