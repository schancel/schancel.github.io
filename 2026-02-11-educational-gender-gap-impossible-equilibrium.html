When 60% of college graduates are women but women prefer partners who earn more, the math doesn’t work—and we did this deliberately

Shammah Chancellor

2026-02-11

There’s a structural problem in the dating market that almost nobody wants to acknowledge: the supply of educated men is insufficient to meet educated women’s mate selection preferences.

This isn’t about morality or what anyone “deserves.” It’s about math. And the math doesn’t work.

The Numbers

As of 2025, women earn approximately 60% of bachelor’s degrees awarded in the United States. Men earn roughly 40%.

This represents a complete reversal from 1970, when men earned 57% of degrees and women 43%. The crossover happened around 1982. The gap has widened every year since.

By 2025, men represent less than 40% of total college graduates.

Women’s Mate Selection Preferences

Women, on average, practice assortative mating: they prefer partners with equal or higher education and income levels.

This isn’t cultural conditioning. It’s evolutionarily rational. Women bear the costs of pregnancy and childrearing. Selecting for partners with resources to support family formation makes biological and economic sense.

Even as women’s education and earning power has increased, the preference for partners who earn equal or more has remained remarkably stable. College-educated women overwhelmingly prefer college-educated male partners. High-earning women prefer partners who earn at least as much.

This preference is not wrong. It’s rational given the realities of childbearing, career interruption for mothers, and economic security needs for raising children.

The Mathematical Impossibility

Here’s where the math breaks down:

  • 60% of college graduates are women
  • Women prefer partners with equal or higher education
  • 40% of college graduates are men

If every college-educated man partnered with exactly one college-educated woman, at least 20% of college-educated women would have no college-educated male partners available.

And that’s the best case—assuming: - Every college-educated man wants to partner - Perfect matching with no preferences beyond education - No men partnering with less-educated women - No men staying in the dating market with multiple partners - Equal age distribution (in reality, women date older, shrinking the pool further)

In reality, the situation is worse:

  • Some college-educated men partner with less-educated women (expanding their options, shrinking women’s)
  • Some men never marry or partner long-term
  • Age gaps mean college-educated women in their 30s are competing for men in their 30s-40s who graduated when the ratio was closer to 50/50 (better for women then, but that cohort is aging out)
  • Geographic clustering (educated people concentrate in cities) means local ratios can be even worse

The supply mathematically cannot meet the demand.

Why Women’s Preferences Can’t Just “Adjust”

The common response: “Women need to be willing to date down in education and income.”

This ignores economic reality.

If a college-educated woman earning $70,000/year wants children, she faces: - Career interruption during pregnancy and early childrearing - Motherhood penalty in wages (averaging 4% per child) - Need for partner income to maintain household during her reduced earning years - Childcare costs averaging $11,000-$20,000 per year per child

A partner who earns significantly less means: - Can’t maintain household on one income during childbearing years - Both parents must work full-time - Childcare costs consume a large portion of the lower-earning partner’s income - Economic stress compounds relationship stress - The economic structure that necessitates dual income makes having children economically punishing

Women’s preference for equal or higher-earning partners isn’t superficial. It’s economically necessary given the costs women bear for childbearing.

Telling college-educated women to “date down” is telling them to either: 1. Abandon having children (eliminates the reason for needing a partner in the first place) 2. Accept severe economic stress during family formation years 3. Have children while both parents work full-time and watch childcare costs consume income

None of these are attractive. All are economically punishing compared to previous generations when single-income families were viable.

How We Got Here: Feminist Policy and Male Educational Collapse

The 60/40 graduation gap didn’t happen by accident. It’s the result of deliberate policy choices over 40+ years.

Title IX and Selective Application

Title IX (1972) prohibited sex discrimination in federally funded education programs. Its application has been remarkably asymmetric:

Where Title IX was enforced aggressively: - College athletics (male sports cut to achieve gender parity in participation) - STEM programs (targeted recruitment, women-only scholarships, support programs) - Sexual harassment/assault (campus tribunals, Obama-era guidance) - Admissions (scrutiny of any gender imbalance favoring men)

Where Title IX was ignored or justified: - Male underrepresentation in college overall (explained as men’s “choice” or “lack of interest”) - Male underrepresentation in humanities, social sciences, education (no targeted recruitment) - Male dropout rates exceeding female dropout rates (no special intervention programs) - Discipline disparities (boys disciplined at higher rates than girls from elementary school on)

The implicit rule: Gender imbalances favoring men require intervention. Gender imbalances favoring women are either ignored or celebrated as progress.

“The Future is Female” as Policy

The 2010s saw explicit policy framing that treated male educational underperformance not as a problem to solve, but as deserved correction of historical injustice.

Obama administration education policy heavily emphasized women’s educational advancement while treating the growing male enrollment gap as either unimportant or evidence of justice. Programs, scholarships, and recruitment efforts targeted women in male-dominated fields. No equivalent programs targeted men in female-dominated fields or addressed male underrepresentation overall.

The implicit message to boys: Your underperformance is either your fault (toxic masculinity, lack of effort) or deserved (payback for historical male advantage). Either way, no systematic support is coming.

Education System Designed for Female Success Patterns

The shift from objective assessment (standardized tests, clear rubrics) to subjective assessment (class participation, group work, emotional intelligence, classroom behavior) systematically favors female behavioral patterns.

  • Boys disciplined more for behavior that doesn’t fit classroom management (fidgeting, physical energy, direct confrontation)
  • Recess and physical education reduced or eliminated (boys need physical activity more than girls for attention regulation)
  • Emphasis on verbal skills and emotional expression (areas where girls develop earlier)
  • De-emphasis on spatial reasoning and mechanical problem-solving (areas where boys often excel)

This isn’t deliberate anti-male bias by individual teachers. It’s structural: the education system optimized for female behavioral patterns, then treated male underperformance as individual failure rather than systemic mismatch.

The Result: Male Educational Collapse

From 1970 to 2025: - Male college enrollment rate: stagnant or declining in most demographics - Female college enrollment rate: dramatic increase - Male dropout rates: higher than female at every education level - Male engagement in K-12: declining across all measures

We didn’t just allow this to happen. We designed systems that produced this outcome, then treated the outcome as either irrelevant or deserved.

Why This Compounds Economic Divergence

This educational gap makes the economic divergence problem even worse:

Already true: - Wages decoupled from productivity after 1971 - Single-income families became economically unviable - Housing, healthcare, education costs rose faster than wages - Provision capacity for men collapsed economically

Educational gap adds: - Fewer men have credentials that predict higher earning potential - College degree increasingly necessary for middle-class income - Men without degrees increasingly locked out of stable employment - The men who could economically provide (college-educated, higher-earning) are insufficient in number to meet demand

The combination is catastrophic:

Not only is provision economically harder (wage stagnation, cost increases), but we’ve systematically reduced the number of men who could even attempt it by denying them the educational credentials that predict earning capacity.

The Impossible Equilibrium

What happens when 60% of college graduates are women but women prefer partners who earn equal or more?

Outcome 1: College-Educated Women Compete for Insufficient Supply

The 40% of college-educated men become a scarce resource. Basic supply and demand:

  • Men’s bargaining power increases (scarcity)
  • Women’s bargaining power decreases (surplus)
  • Men can be more selective, maintain more options, commit less
  • Women compete harder, accept worse terms, tolerate worse behavior
  • Power dynamics shift heavily toward men who meet criteria

This explains why college-educated women report difficulty finding partners while college-educated men often report abundance of options.

Outcome 2: Women Partner with Less-Educated Men

Some women adjust preferences and partner with men who earn less or lack degrees.

The problems: - Economic stress when children require one parent reducing work - Resentment if woman earns more but still bears childrearing burden - Stability issues if less-educated men face higher unemployment risk - Lower relationship satisfaction (studies show women who out-earn husbands report lower marital satisfaction)

Outcome 3: Women Don’t Partner at All

Increasingly, college-educated women opt out of marriage and children entirely.

From their perspective, this is rational: - Can’t find partners who meet economic criteria for stable family formation - Dual-income requirement makes having children economically punishing - Career advancement requires not taking childbearing years off - Single life maintains autonomy and economic stability

But this produces demographic collapse: - College-educated women have lowest fertility rates (averaging <1.5 children) - Below replacement rate (2.1) - Compounds across generations - Exactly the pattern that preceded Rome’s decline

All three outcomes are happening simultaneously. And none of them produce functional family formation at scale.

What This Means Practically

If you’re a college-educated woman in 2025:

The math is against you.

At least 20% of college-educated women cannot find college-educated male partners even if every college-educated man partners with exactly one woman. In reality, the percentage unable to find what they’re selecting for is much higher.

You can: 1. Compete harder for the insufficient supply of college-educated men (accept worse terms, tolerate worse behavior, less commitment) 2. Adjust preferences downward (partner with less-educated/lower-earning men, accept economic stress and lower stability) 3. Opt out entirely (don’t partner, don’t have children, maintain autonomy)

All three are individually rational responses to structural impossibility. None of them produce the stable family formation that women actually want.

If you’re a college-educated man in 2025:

You have structural bargaining power from scarcity. You can be more selective, maintain options longer, commit less readily. Many men are doing exactly this.

But this compounds the coordination failure. The abundance of options reduces incentive to commit to any one relationship. The ability to maintain multiple options simultaneously destroys trust. The structural advantage produces behavior patterns that prevent the stable relationships men claim to want eventually.

Both genders responding rationally to structural incentives produces collective catastrophe.

Why This Won’t Be Fixed

The same public choice theory that prevents economic reforms prevents educational reforms:

Who benefits from current system: - Education bureaucracy (more female teachers/administrators, programs designed for female success) - Feminist advocacy organizations (can point to women’s educational attainment as victory) - Colleges themselves (women are better customers: higher graduation rates, less disciplinary issues, more engaged) - Politicians signaling support for women’s advancement

Who bears the costs: - Individual men (lower educational attainment, reduced earning potential) - Individual women (unable to find partners meeting mate selection criteria) - Future children (never born because family formation is economically impossible) - Civilization (demographic collapse, infrastructure decay, below-replacement fertility)

The political economy:

Concentrated benefits (education industry, advocacy groups, political signaling) versus dispersed costs (individuals suffering, long-term civilizational decline).

Any politician proposing programs to address male educational underperformance faces: - Accusations of prioritizing men over women - Opposition from feminist advocacy groups - Charges of rolling back hard-won progress - Electoral punishment from women voters

Meanwhile, the costs are born by: - Men who didn’t go to college (disorganized, no political power) - Women who can’t find partners (blame themselves or men individually, not the system) - Children who are never born (no constituency)

Reform is politically impossible until crisis forces it.

The Historical Pattern

We’ve seen this before.

Rome faced a similar crisis: declining marriage rates, below-replacement fertility, gender imbalance from female infanticide. Augustus tried to fix it with laws penalizing the unmarried and rewarding childbearing.

It didn’t work. The structural incentives were too broken. Rome’s population didn’t recover its previous levels until nearly 800 years after the collapse.

We’re running the same experiment with the variables reversed: Instead of killing female children and creating male surplus, we’re systematically advantaging women in education and creating female surplus in the college-educated dating pool.

The result is the same: coordination failure, demographic decline, civilizational collapse.

The Uncomfortable Truth

We deliberately created an educational system that produces male underperformance, celebrated the results as progress, and ignored the predictable consequences for family formation.

Now college-educated women can’t find college-educated men to partner with because we systematically ensured there wouldn’t be enough of them.

This wasn’t accidental. This was policy. “The future is female” wasn’t just a slogan—it was a blueprint. We implemented it through Title IX asymmetry, education reform favoring female behavioral patterns, celebration of male educational collapse as deserved justice, and complete refusal to address male underperformance systemically.

And now we’re living with the consequences:

  • College-educated women competing for insufficient supply of college-educated men
  • Relationship market dynamics heavily favoring the scarce resource (educated men)
  • Women either compromising preferences, competing harder for worse terms, or opting out entirely
  • Below-replacement fertility among college-educated women
  • Demographic collapse compounding across generations

The math doesn’t work. The equilibrium is impossible. And we did this deliberately.

The question isn’t whether this system will collapse. It’s how long we pretend it’s sustainable before admitting we destroyed the coordination mechanisms that made family formation possible.
