Where Is the Second Witness?

He wanders through darkness, his feet stumble on stone.

He fumbles for light but grasps only shadow and silence.

He seeks the Light of the World, yet finds only night— deep, endless night.

Where are the burning wicks? Where is the oil that never fails? Where are flint and steel and sacred spark?

The hills lie dark—where is the city set on a hill?

His love grows cold as winter in his bones.

He knows faithful saints remain, scattered like distant stars— few, flickering, but burning still. Yet where are their lamps? Why does darkness swallow their light?

O, if they could be gathered, drawn close together— they would kindle flame across all the earth.

But one question haunts the shadows: When the Son of Man comes, will He find faith on earth?

Where is the second witness?

The Bride Is Not Ready

Scripture declares with eschatological certainty: “The marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready” (Revelation 19:7 ESV). Yet this prophetic perfect tense speaks of what will be, not what is.

Has the Bride made herself ready? The question haunts our comfortable pews.

Jesus told a parable about ten virgins waiting for the bridegroom—a story that cuts to the heart of readiness versus mere waiting. Five were wise, understanding that preparation requires substance. They brought oil for their lamps. Five were foolish, mistaking attendance for readiness. They brought no oil.

When the bridegroom came at midnight, the foolish virgins discovered that good intentions cannot fuel dying flames. They scrambled to find oil, but preparation cannot be purchased in the moment of need. The bridegroom arrived, and those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast.

The door was shut.

The foolish virgins came later, crying “Lord, lord, open to us!” But relationship cannot be claimed where preparation has been refused. His answer: “Truly, I say to you, I do not know you” (Matthew 25:1-12 ESV).

The contemporary church says “Come, Lord Jesus” (Revelation 22:20) with liturgical fervor. We sing songs about His return with emotional intensity. We preach sermons about the end times with prophetic certainty. We wait for Him to come with eschatological anticipation.

But are we preparing? Or have we become the foolish virgins—waiting without oil, calling “Lord, Lord” while remaining unprepared for the Bridegroom’s actual arrival?

Waiting, Not Preparing

John the Baptist cried in the wilderness with prophetic urgency: “Prepare the way of the Lord, make his paths straight” (Matthew 3:3 ESV).

That was his mission—to prepare the way for Christ’s first advent. A forerunner’s work: making crooked paths straight, rough places smooth, hearts ready for the Messiah’s arrival.

But who is preparing the way for His return? Where is the second witness?

The contemporary church has become expert at waiting—a passive anticipation that substitutes hope for action. We wait for Christ to return while doing nothing to prepare the way. We wait for God to fix injustice “when Jesus comes back” as though the Kingdom’s manifestation requires no participation from the King’s subjects. We wait for the Kingdom to come while refusing to embody its values.

But Scripture does not call us merely to wait. Waiting is the posture of those who cannot act. Scripture calls us to prepare—the active work of those who understand their role in the divine drama.

Paul wrote with apostolic urgency: “Besides this you know the time, that the hour has come for you to wake from sleep. For salvation is nearer to us now than when we first believed. The night is far gone; the day is at hand” (Romans 13:11-12 ESV).

Wake from sleep. The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

Yet the church sleeps on, mistaking doctrinal orthodoxy for spiritual readiness, confusing eschatological knowledge with eschatological preparation.

Jesus warned us with prophetic precision: “Because lawlessness will be increased, the love of many will grow cold” (Matthew 24:12 ESV).

Perhaps this is that time. Perhaps this is why brothers pass by brothers in need, their hearts grown cold toward the household of faith. Perhaps this is why the lamps have gone dark—not from persecution, but from the slow cooling of love itself.

O Church, O Church

He wept over His city with divine grief: “O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets and stones those who are sent to it! How often would I have gathered your children together as a hen gathers her brood under her wings, and you were not willing!” (Matthew 23:37 ESV).

The lament echoes through history—divine love met with systematic refusal, protection offered to those determined to remain exposed, gathering attempted for those committed to scattering.

Would He grieve now with the same divine anguish: O Church, O Church—how long will you sleep through your own hour of visitation?

How long will you wait in spiritual passivity while preparation demands spiritual activity? How long will you say “Lord, Lord” with liturgical precision while refusing to do what He commands with life-transforming obedience?

Scripture cries out with prophetic urgency: “Awake, O sleeper, and arise from the dead, and Christ will shine on you” (Ephesians 5:14 ESV).

The call is both present imperative and eschatological necessity—wake now, for the light is already breaking. Yet the church sleeps through its own resurrection moment. And the lights remain dark.

And he wanders in darkness, searching for even one lamp burning among the people of Light, finding only cold wicks and empty vessels where the oil of active faith should burn bright.

Faith Without Works Is Dead

Jesus asked the ultimate eschatological question: “When the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on earth?” (Luke 18:8 ESV).

James answers with theological precision that cuts through centuries of spiritual self-deception:

“What good is it, my brothers, if someone says he has faith but does not have works? Can that faith save him? If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food, and one of you says to them, ‘Go in peace, be warmed and filled,’ without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that? So also faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead” (James 2:14-17 ESV).

Dead faith. Empty orthodoxy. Spiritual words without material substance. “Go in peace, be warmed and filled”—the religious dismissal that substitutes blessing for burden-bearing, sentiment for service, spiritual platitudes for practical obedience. No oil for the lamp. No preparation for the Bridegroom’s coming.

If Christ returned today, would He find faith on earth? The question grows more urgent as the gap between confession and conduct widens.

Or would He find a church that says “Lord, Lord” with doctrinal precision but does not do what He says with practical obedience? A church that sings songs about His return with emotional fervor but has not made herself ready through faithful action? A church waiting for the Kingdom’s arrival but refusing to prepare the way through Kingdom living?

Here lies the fundamental contradiction: How can the King return to subjects who do not obey the King? How can the Bridegroom arrive to find a Bride who has not made herself ready?

What Does It Mean to Prepare the Way?

This is not merely prophetic lament. Lament without hope is despair; lament with hope is intercession. There is still time.

The Bridegroom has not yet come. The door is not yet shut. There remains space for preparation—but only if we understand what preparation actually means, not what we have sentimentalized it to mean.

What does it mean to prepare the way of the Lord? The question demands precision, not pious generalities.

Jesus Himself told us with crystalline clarity: “Not everyone who says to me, ‘Lord, Lord,’ will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven” (Matthew 7:21 ESV).

The answer cuts through religious rhetoric: Doing. Active obedience to the will of the Father. Not merely saying “Lord, Lord” with theological correctness.

Preparing the way means embodying Kingdom reality in advance of Kingdom manifestation:

  • Obeying the King we claim to serve—allegiance proven through action
  • Ensuring our faith produces works, or acknowledging our faith remains dead
  • Filling our lamps with the oil of active obedience
  • Stopping our passive waiting and starting our Kingdom acting

Scripture is not unclear about what God commands. The ambiguity lies not in the commands but in our willingness to face their implications. The question is not interpretive complexity but moral courage: Will we obey the clear commands of the King we claim to serve?

If Christ Is King, We Obey

Here lies the fundamental theological inconsistency that undermines our entire eschatological hope: We confess Christ as “King” while living as autonomous subjects.

Peter identifies the church’s true political identity: “a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession” (1 Peter 2:9 ESV).

Notice the political terminology: royal priesthood, holy nation. This is not metaphorical language but political reality. We are citizens of a Kingdom, members of a royal priesthood, subjects of a divine King.

If Christ is truly our King—not merely our Savior, not merely our Lord, but our actual reigning King—then His commands cannot be reduced to suggestions requiring our interpretive approval. They are the royal decrees of the King to whom we have pledged allegiance.

Jesus Himself exposed this fundamental contradiction with devastating simplicity: “Why do you call me ‘Lord, Lord,’ and not do what I tell you?” (Luke 6:46 ESV).

Consider the political implications: If subjects systematically ignore their king’s commands, who can honestly claim that king truly reigns over them? A king whose subjects treat his decrees as optional suggestions isn’t functionally king at all—regardless of the titles they use to address him.

Allegiance to a king means obeying the king’s commands. Anything less reduces kingship to ceremonial status and allegiance to empty words.

Direct Commands, Not Interpretive Applications

Let me be clear about what I’m addressing: Jesus’s direct, imperative commands to individuals.

Not someone’s interpretation of a parable applied to collective political action. Not guilt-based activism about systemic issues. Not debates about immigration policy or other causes where Christians disagree on application.

I’m talking about clear, explicit commands that Jesus gave to His followers about how you personally should act when you personally are asked for help by a brother or sister in Christ.

For example: “Give to the one who begs from you, and do not refuse the one who would borrow from you” (Matthew 5:42 ESV).

That’s not ambiguous. It’s not a parable requiring interpretation. It’s a direct imperative.

These are individual commands about personal obedience—not collective mandates about political platforms.

There’s no interpretation debate needed. Jesus said it. The question is: Do we obey?

Christians First

Here we encounter a profound theological truth that contemporary Christianity has systematically obscured: The New Testament explicitly commands Christians to prioritize helping other Christians.

Paul articulates this principle with apostolic authority: “So then, as we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to those who are of the household of faith” (Galatians 6:10 ESV).

Notice the precise formulation: universal benevolence (“to everyone”) balanced by covenant priority (“especially the household of faith”). This is not narrow sectarianism but theological realism about the nature of covenant relationships.

Paul reinforces this elsewhere: “Contribute to the needs of the saints” (Romans 12:13 ESV). The saints—fellow believers bound together in the body of Christ.

John grounds this priority in the very nature of Christian love: “By this we know love, that he laid down his life for us, and we ought to lay down our lives for the brothers. But if anyone has the world’s goods and sees his brother in need, yet closes his heart against him, how does God’s love abide in him?” (1 John 3:16-17 ESV).

James’s paradigmatic example follows the same pattern: “If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food…” (James 2:15 ESV).

The early church embodied this understanding without apology. Luke records their remarkable experiment in Christian community:

“And all who believed were together and had all things in common. And they were selling their possessions and belongings and distributing the proceeds to all, as any had need.” (Acts 2:44-45 ESV)

“There was not a needy person among them, for as many as were owners of lands or houses sold them and brought the proceeds of what was sold and laid it at the apostles’ feet, and it was distributed to each as any had need.” (Acts 4:34-35 ESV)

Notice the careful boundaries: “All who believed.” “Among them.” The church was caring for the church—not exclusively, but primarily.

This is not exclusion—it is covenant fidelity expressed through practical triage.

When resources are finite and needs are infinite, love requires wisdom about priority and proximity. When a house burns, you save your children before your neighbors’ children—not because you value neighbors less, but because covenant relationships create primary obligations. Emergency responders follow triage protocols, treating the most critical patients first—not from indifference to others, but from wisdom about maximizing good with limited resources.

The Good Samaritan helped the beaten man sacrificially—but he also maintained his own ability to travel, work, and help again. Prioritization is not indifference; it is wisdom that ensures sustainable compassion.

Yet contemporary Christianity has inverted this biblical priority with devastating results.

Churches raise millions for distant mission projects while believers in their own community struggle with rent, medical bills, and basic needs. Christian families lose their homes to foreclosure while their congregations fund new buildings in other countries. Believing single mothers cannot afford groceries, but the same congregations that pass them by will enthusiastically support feeding programs overseas.

This is not wisdom—it is disobedience disguised as virtue, covenant infidelity masquerading as universal compassion.

Wise Stewardship, Not Pathological Self-Destruction

None of this means we help foolishly or destroy our own families in the process.

Paul is clear: “But if anyone does not provide for his relatives, and especially for members of his household, he has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever” (1 Timothy 5:8 ESV).

You must provide for your household first. If you give away so much that your own family suffers, you’ve violated a direct command. This isn’t selfishness—it’s obedience.

Jesus Himself lamented that “the sons of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own generation than the sons of light” (Luke 16:8 ESV).

We’re called to be wise, not foolish. Jesus told His disciples: “Behold, I am sending you out as sheep in the midst of wolves, so be wise as serpents and innocent as doves” (Matthew 10:16 ESV).

Wise as serpents. We should help with wisdom and discernment.

Paul also writes: “If anyone is not willing to work, let him not eat” (2 Thessalonians 3:10 ESV). There’s a difference between someone in genuine need and someone who refuses to work.

Wisdom and discernment matter.

The Good Samaritan is instructive here. He saw a man beaten and left for dead—genuine need. He gave immediate help (bandaged wounds, put him on his own animal). He took him to an inn and paid for his care. He even promised to cover additional expenses.

But notice what he didn’t do: He didn’t give the man everything he owned. He didn’t destroy his own ability to travel and work.

He gave generously and wisely.

That’s the model: Help genuinely, sacrificially, but not pathologically.

“But Grace Is Sufficient!”

The anticipated objection rings with theological certainty: “We’re saved by grace through faith, not by works. Grace is sufficient!”

Indeed. Paul’s formulation has anchored Protestant theology for five centuries: “For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast” (Ephesians 2:8-9 ESV).

This truth stands unassailable. But theological precision requires reading the complete argument: “For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them” (Ephesians 2:10 ESV).

We are not saved BY works—we are saved FOR works. This distinction is not semantic; it is soteriological. Grace initiates salvation, but grace also defines salvation’s purpose: the restoration of human beings to their created function as agents of God’s good works.

Jesus Himself establishes the theological framework wherein divine grace and human obedience operate in dynamic relationship. When teaching about forgiveness, He articulates a principle that extends far beyond forgiveness itself:

“For if you forgive others their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you, but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses” (Matthew 6:14-15 ESV).

This is not salvation by works but salvation that necessarily produces works. Jesus consistently connects divine action toward us with human action toward others—not as meritorious cause but as evidential fruit. Our obedience does not earn God’s favor; it demonstrates the reality of God’s transforming grace within us.

Consider the parable of the two sons:

“A man had two sons. And he went to the first and said, ‘Son, go and work in the vineyard today.’ And he answered, ‘I will not,’ but afterward he changed his mind and went. And he went to the other son and said the same. And he answered, ‘I will, sir,’ but did not go. Which of the two did the will of his father?” They said, “The first.” (Matthew 21:28-31 ESV)

The son who said he would obey but didn’t was disobedient. The son who did the father’s will was obedient—regardless of what he initially said.

Jesus’s verdict? “Truly, I say to you, the tax collectors and the prostitutes go into the kingdom of God before you” (Matthew 21:31 ESV).

Abraham’s Faith Was Active Faith

The theological objection continues: “But what about Abraham? His faith was counted to him as righteousness (Genesis 15:6). That’s faith alone!”

Indeed. Paul employs Abraham as the paradigmatic example of justification by faith: “For what does the Scripture say? ‘Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness’” (Romans 4:3 ESV).

But what kind of faith did Abraham actually possess? Here theological precision becomes essential.

James provides the crucial analysis: “Was not Abraham our father justified by works when he offered up his son Isaac on the altar? You see that faith was active along with his works, and faith was completed by his works; and the Scripture was fulfilled that says, ‘Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness’—and he was called a friend of God” (James 2:21-23 ESV).

Paul and James are not engaged in theological contradiction but theological complementarity. They describe different aspects of the same spiritual reality:

  • Paul: Abraham was justified by faith (forensic declaration of righteousness)
  • James: That justifying faith was active faith, producing works (evidential validation of genuine faith)

Abraham’s faith was completed by works—not perfected in the sense of being improved, but brought to its intended fulfillment. When God called him to leave Ur, he left. “By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to receive as an inheritance. And he went out, not knowing where he was going” (Hebrews 11:8 ESV).

His faith caused motion, produced obedience, generated action. This was not mere intellectual assent to propositions about God but trust in God that necessarily expressed itself through life-transforming obedience. That is the faith that was counted as righteousness.

James concludes with anatomical precision: “For as the body apart from the spirit is dead, so also faith apart from works is dead” (James 2:26 ESV).

Dead faith possesses no saving power. And dead faith—faith without works, confession without obedience—is what Christ may find when He returns to His sleeping church.

What Jesus Says Matters

At the final judgment, what will Jesus say matters?

He tells us in Matthew 25:

“Then the King will say to those on his right, ‘Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me drink, I was a stranger and you welcomed me, I was naked and you clothed me, I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me.’” (Matthew 25:34-36 ESV)

The righteous are confused—when did they see Jesus in need? His answer:

“Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me.” (Matthew 25:40 ESV)

Notice: “My brothers.” This is about how we treat fellow believers.

And those on the left? They didn’t help when they saw need. Jesus’s verdict: “Depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels” (Matthew 25:41 ESV).

This isn’t a side issue. Jesus puts helping those in need at the center of final judgment.

We Are the Body of Christ

“But why doesn’t God just help them Himself?”

Because we are “the body of Christ, and individually members of it” (1 Corinthians 12:27 ESV).

God doesn’t have other hands than ours.

When we defer action to a “magic God” who will somehow help apart from His people acting, we misunderstand how God has chosen to work in the world.

Through His church. Through His people. Through us.

If we don’t act, He doesn’t act—not because He’s limited, but because this is the means He’s ordained.

We are His hands and feet. We are the lights of the world—but the lights have gone out.

The Disconnect

Somehow we’ve convinced ourselves that:

  • Prayer substitutes for action
  • God will handle need “when Jesus returns”
  • We’re not responsible when a brother or sister directly asks for help
  • Warm feelings and good intentions count as obedience
  • Calling Christ “Lord” doesn’t require doing what He says
  • Grace means obedience is optional
  • Helping distant strangers is more important than helping struggling believers in our own community

These are the cold wicks. These are the empty vessels. This is why the lights have gone out.

But that’s not how kingdoms work. And it’s not what Scripture teaches.

Jesus warned: “Not everyone who says to me, ‘Lord, Lord,’ will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven” (Matthew 7:21 ESV).

Doing. The will. Of the Father.

Not just saying “Lord, Lord.”

What Repentance Looks Like

If you’re reading this and feeling the weight of prophetic conviction, the question becomes profoundly practical: What does repentance actually look like beyond emotional response?

Not another program to implement. Not a systematic approach to manage. Not another church activity to schedule.

Repentance looks like this—simple, concrete, immediate:

Recognize need when you encounter it. Not every need constitutes your calling, but when a brother or sister in Christ approaches you directly for help—recognize it as a divine appointment. Don’t look away through spiritual avoidance. Don’t spiritualize material need into immaterial “prayer requests.” See it with the eyes that Christ would use.

Keep margin for Kingdom interruptions. If you’ve structured your finances so tightly that you cannot respond when asked, you’ve structured your life to preclude obedience. God commands generosity. Generosity requires margin. “From everyone who has been given much, much will be demanded” (Luke 12:48 ESV).

Live interruptibly. The Good Samaritan was traveling with purpose. He had destinations and deadlines. But he stopped when love required it. Preparing the way means holding our plans lightly enough that the needs of brothers and sisters can interrupt them.

Respond when asked. Not “I’ll pray about it” as pious deflection. Not “God will provide” when God has already provided through placing you in the path of need. When a brother or sister asks and you possess the means—give. That is the command, not the suggestion.

Start small, but start now. You need not solve every problem or help every person. But when someone in the household of faith asks you directly, and you have the means—that is your moment of testing. That is your specific obedience.

This requires no theological complexity. It demands no advanced degrees. It necessitates no mission trips to distant lands.

It is simply this: A brother asks. You have the means. You give.

That’s what the lamps and oil look like at human scale—not grand gestures but faithful responses to immediate need.

Will You Prepare the Way?

He returns to where he began: wandering through darkness, unable to see because the lights that should illumine the path have gone out.

Jesus declared with prophetic authority: “You are the light of the world” (Matthew 5:14 ESV). But where is that light when darkness covers the earth?

He asked the ultimate eschatological question: “When the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on earth?” (Luke 18:8 ESV).

The answer depends entirely on whether we prepare the way. Whether we wake from our eschatological slumber. Whether our faith produces the works that prove it lives, or remains the dead orthodoxy that saves no one.

The hour grows late, and the night deepens. Paul wrote with apostolic urgency: “The night is far gone; the day is at hand” (Romans 13:12 ESV). The Bridegroom may arrive at any moment. The door of preparation may soon be shut.

Are you ready when honesty strips away religious pretense? Has the Bride truly made herself ready, or have we mistaken liturgical correctness for eschatological preparation? Do you have oil for your lamp, or only the empty vessel of good intentions?

Will you be among the wise virgins who were ready when the Bridegroom came? Or will you join the foolish virgins crying “Lord, lord, open to us!” only to hear the most terrifying words in Scripture: “Truly, I say to you, I do not know you”?

You Cannot Prepare Corporately

The church as a whole may fail in its eschatological preparation. The lights may remain dark across vast portions of Christendom. Most may sleep through their hour of visitation until the Bridegroom comes.

Jesus Himself suggested this sobering possibility when He asked, “Will he find faith on earth?” The question implies the real possibility of a negative answer.

You cannot control whether the corporate church awakens from its slumber. You cannot force oil into others’ empty lamps. You cannot make the institutional body obey the commands of its professed King.

But you can prepare. You can be light in the darkness that surrounds you. You can ensure your lamp has oil when the Bridegroom arrives.

The ultimate question is not whether the church as a whole will be ready—that outcome may already be determined. The question is whether YOU will be ready when He comes.

When a brother or sister in Christ asks you for help, and you possess the means—will you give with open hands? Or will you offer the empty benediction “Go in peace, be warmed and filled” while closing your heart to their actual need?

When Christ commands “Give to the one who begs from you”—will you obey your professed King? Or will you discover theological reasons to refuse the clear command of Scripture?

When Scripture prioritizes “especially those who are of the household of faith”—will you help struggling believers first? Or will you ignore covenant obligations while funding photogenic distant causes?

This Is How You Prepare the Way

Preparing the way for Christ’s return means embodying Kingdom reality before Kingdom manifestation:

  • Obeying the King you claim to serve—proving allegiance through action
  • Helping brothers and sisters in genuine need—covenant fidelity in practice
  • Maintaining faith that produces works—living faith, not dead orthodoxy
  • Being light in the darkness—illuminating the path for others
  • Having oil for your lamp when the Bridegroom comes—preparation proven by readiness

You may not be able to wake the entire sleeping church. You may continue to wander through darkness, surrounded by extinguished lamps and cold wicks.

But your lamp can burn bright. You can be found faithful when He returns. Your oil can be sufficient for the long night of waiting.

Scattered across the landscape are small communities of Christians who take these commands seriously—house churches, intentional communities, brothers and sisters who actually live as the body of Christ rather than merely attending its meetings. They are rare, but they exist. They prove it’s possible.

The early church showed us the pattern: “There was not a needy person among them” (Acts 4:34 ESV).

It can be that way again. Perhaps not for the institutional church as a whole—that may be beyond recovery. But for you, and those who join you in radical obedience to the clear commands of the King.

When the Son of Man Comes

Jesus asked the question that haunts every generation: “When the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on earth?” (Luke 18:8 ESV).

Would we recognize Him if He returned today—not in the clouds with angelic hosts, but in the brother asking for help, the sister facing eviction, the believer choosing between medicine and groceries?

The answer for the church as a whole? He fears he knows. The lamps have gone dark. The oil has been poured out on distant causes while brothers and sisters stumble in darkness. Love has grown cold through the increase of lawlessness, just as He warned.

But the answer for you remains unwritten. That depends entirely on what you do next.

Will you prepare the way of the Lord through obedience to the King? Will you ensure your faith produces works that prove it lives? Will you fill your lamp with the oil of active love for the household of faith?

Or will you continue the comfortable pattern of waiting without preparing, saying “Lord, Lord” with theological precision while doing nothing that He commands, until the Bridegroom comes and finds you exactly as you are—unprepared, with empty vessels and cold wicks?

The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

Awake, O sleeper. Arise from the dead. Let Christ shine on you.

Prepare the way of the Lord.
