A Request for Support

Dear friends,

I hope this letter finds you well. I’m writing to a small circle of Christian friends because I’m in a season of genuine financial need, and I’m asking for help.

This is awkward. I’ve spent my life building things, solving problems, and avoiding dependence on others. But sometimes faithfulness means asking, even when it’s uncomfortable.

First, if you’re stretched thin yourself, please don’t feel pressure from this letter. Many of us are carrying real burdens right now. If this isn’t your season to give, you remain my friend—full stop. I mean that.

For those who might be able to help, let me share what’s happening and what I’m asking for.

What I’m asking: If you’re able, I’m requesting modest recurring support—$8/month is the sweet spot, though any amount helps. The simplest way is a paid subscription to my blog, but any form that works for you is welcome.

I’m specifically asking for small amounts from many people rather than large gifts from a few. The goal is 100-125 supporters at $8/month—enough to bridge to sustainability without anyone carrying too heavy a load.

If you can’t give financially, I’d be grateful if you’d share this with others who might be in a season to help. And please do pray for me—that matters deeply.

The situation: Over the past few years, I’ve been hit by a perfect storm—severe flooding damage to my home (uninsured), a business collapse during the 2023 interest rate shock, and a layoff. These wiped out twenty years of savings. I’m underwater on my house, and as a software engineer, I’m navigating the fastest disruption my field has ever seen from AI.

I have $2,000/month in fixed income. In the Bay Area, that’s not livable—basic survival here costs $3,500-4,500 monthly. I’ve looked at lower-cost US locations, but even there, $2,000/month leaves me in crisis mode. Southeast Asia (Thailand or Vietnam) offers something different: $3,000/month provides comfortable, sustainable living with excellent infrastructure, strong expat communities, and the peace of mind to think beyond survival.

The plan: My fixed income ($2,000/month) plus modest support ($1,000/month from roughly 100-125 people) would let me relocate to a sustainable situation. This isn’t indefinite rescue—it’s bridge funding to get from an unsustainable context to one where I can write, build tools, and explore serving others with my technical skills without constant financial crisis.

I’m actively pursuing remote work and freelance opportunities. This bridge would mean stability while I rebuild rather than treading water in an unsustainable location.

A note on Scripture: I’ve thought a lot about Matthew 5:42—“Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you.” I’ve tried to live by that in small ways. Now I’m on the receiving end, which is humbling and uncomfortable. I share that not to pressure anyone, but because it’s been meaningful to me. Your own discernment matters most.

Whether you give or not, please pray for me—for wisdom, steadiness, and clarity about faithfulness in this season.

If you do support, I receive it with deep gratitude. If you can’t, or if this doesn’t feel right to you, nothing changes between us. I mean that.

Thank you for reading this and for being part of my life.

In friendship and faith, Shammah
