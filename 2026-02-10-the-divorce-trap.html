How We Optimized for Adult Autonomy and Destroyed Children’s Attachment Security

Shammah Chancellor

2026-02-10

There’s a coordination problem at the heart of modern marriage that almost nobody wants to talk about honestly: we can’t figure out how to make divorce possible when genuinely necessary without making it so easy that it destroys commitment and damages children at scale.

The pre-1970s system trapped people in genuinely bad marriages. The post-1970s system creates attachment disorders in millions of children. Both are failures, but we treat the second as if it’s success because we only measure adult autonomy, not children’s developmental outcomes.

This isn’t a conservative plea to “return to traditional marriage.” The old system had real problems. But the solution we implemented—no-fault unilateral divorce—solved those problems by creating something measurably worse for the humans who didn’t get to choose: the children.

What No-Fault Divorce Was Supposed to Fix

Before no-fault divorce, ending a marriage required proving fault—adultery, abuse, abandonment, or other serious violations. This created real problems:

Trapped people in genuinely bad situations. If your spouse was emotionally abusive but not physically violent, or if you simply grew incompatible over decades, you had no legal exit. Marriages that should have ended persisted for years, sometimes causing serious psychological damage.

Forced public airing of private failures. Proving fault meant courtroom battles over intimate details. The adversarial process escalated conflict, made reconciliation impossible, and turned marital breakdown into public spectacle.

Created perverse incentives. Since fault affected outcomes, spouses had incentives to manufacture or exaggerate claims. False accusations of abuse or infidelity became strategic divorce tactics.

Punished the honest. If you simply weren’t compatible, if you’d grown apart without anyone being at fault, you either stayed married or lied to get out. The system rewarded dishonesty and punished mutual recognition that a marriage wasn’t working.

These were real problems. The reformers who pushed for no-fault divorce weren’t wrong about the system being broken. Where they went catastrophically wrong was in failing to consider the second-order effects on children’s attachment development.

What Happened Next: The Data

California implemented no-fault divorce in 1970. Other states followed rapidly. The effects were immediate and dramatic:

Divorce rates more than doubled. From 9.2 divorces per 1,000 married women in 1960 to 22.6 in 1980. Less than 20% of couples married in 1950 divorced; about 50% of couples married in 1970 divorced.

Half of children born in the 1970s experienced parental divorce, compared to only 11% born in the 1950s.

This wasn’t a gradual cultural shift. It was a legal change that produced immediate, massive increases in family dissolution during children’s critical developmental years.

The Attachment Disaster We Created

Here’s what the divorce reformers didn’t account for: children’s attachment systems develop during the first 5-7 years of life through consistent, predictable interactions with primary caregivers. Disrupting those relationships during the critical period doesn’t just cause temporary distress—it alters the neurological wiring that governs how they’ll approach relationships for the rest of their lives.

The research on divorce and attachment is unambiguous:

Maternal sadness and instability related to divorce are associated with less availability and greater inconsistency in responses. Children implement hyperactivation strategies—constantly seeking attention and reassurance—characteristic of anxious attachment.

Disorganized attachment is seen in children whose parents are inconsistently available, sometimes a source of love and comfort, other times a source of neglect and unavailability. This is exactly what happens when children shuttle between two households with different parenting styles, different rules, different emotional availability.

Frequent overnights of very young children in two homes are associated with attachment insecurity and less regulated behaviors. During the critical period when attachment patterns form, these children experience radical inconsistency in caregiving.

Parental insecurity was most pronounced when parental divorce took place in early childhood, consistent with sensitive periods in attachment development.

The outcomes for these children as adults are measurable and grim:

Adult children of divorce show lower well-being, higher divorce rates, poor marital quality, less positive parent-child relationships, and more insecure attachment styles than adult children from non-divorced families.

The Intergenerational Cascade

The problem compounds across generations. Attachment is transmitted from one generation to the next, and adult attachment predicts the security of children’s attachment relationships with their parents.

Ainsworth’s foundational research established approximately 28-33% insecure attachment in intact American families during the 1970s—right as no-fault divorce was being implemented.

By 1988, college students showed 51.02% insecure attachment—a 21 percentage point increase. These were children born 1966-1970 who experienced the divorce spike firsthand.

By 2011, college students showed 58.38% insecure attachment—a 28 percentage point increase from baseline. These were children born 1989-1993, the second generation raised by divorce-affected parents who struggled to form stable relationships themselves.

We went from 30% insecure to 51% insecure to 58% insecure across two generations. The timeline matches perfectly: divorce implementation damaged the first generation; that damage transmitted intergenerationally, accelerating the collapse.

This is what optimizing for adult autonomy while ignoring children’s attachment needs produces at scale.

The False Choice We’re Offered

The debate gets framed as a binary: either accept no-fault divorce and its consequences, or return to fault-based divorce and trap people in bad marriages.

This is a false choice. There are better systems possible, but they require accepting uncomfortable tradeoffs that neither conservatives nor progressives want to face.

What a Better System Would Look Like

A divorce system that takes children’s attachment security seriously would have these features:

1. Mutual Consent Is Straightforward

When both parties agree the marriage isn’t working, make dissolution simple and non-adversarial. No fault-finding needed when there’s consensus. The goal is minimizing damage to children through cooperative dissolution, not punishing adults for relationship failure.

2. Unilateral Exit Scales With Children’s Developmental Needs

The barriers to unilateral exit should reflect the stakes for children’s attachment development:

No children, or children past attachment formation (8+ years): Lower barriers. Adults have more autonomy when children’s critical developmental windows have passed or don’t exist.

Children ages 0-7 (attachment formation period): Higher barriers. Mandatory waiting periods (6-12 months), required mediation, demonstrated good-faith attempts at reconciliation. The parent wanting out bears more burden of proof and process cost because the stakes for children’s lifelong attachment security are highest.

This isn’t about “saving marriages” morally. It’s about protecting children’s neurological development during critical periods. After age 7-8, when attachment patterns are largely set, the calculus changes.

3. Presumptive Equal Custody

Default to 50/50 physical custody unless clear evidence shows otherwise. Burden of proof on the parent seeking primary custody.

This removes the strategic incentive to file first or make accusations to gain custody advantage. It protects children’s attachment to both parents. And it eliminates the gendered custody presumptions that currently punish fathers and reward strategic behavior.

4. Fault Still Matters for Outcomes

Documented abuse, abandonment, serious addiction, infidelity—these should affect custody arrangements and financial settlements.

Not because we’re moralizing, but because: (a) children’s safety requires it, and (b) creating accountability for destructive behavior reduces the incentive to engage in it.

This requires actual evidence, not just accusations. False allegations should carry serious consequences, including custody impact.

5. Community Mediation Before Legal Process

Require good-faith attempts at reconciliation through neutral mediators before allowing unilateral divorce filing. Not religious counseling (unless both choose it), but trained community mediators who can assess whether reconciliation is genuinely impossible or if the couple is giving up prematurely.

This establishes that exit requires demonstrating genuine effort to fix what’s broken. It reduces frivolous divorces while allowing necessary exits.

Critics will say this “forces people to stay in bad marriages.” No—it requires people to try before leaving. If the marriage is genuinely unsalvageable, mediation will establish that. If it’s premature giving up, mediation might prevent unnecessary dissolution.

6. Fast Path for Documented Serious Harm

Clear evidence of physical abuse, severe untreated addiction, criminal behavior, genuine safety threats—these trigger expedited process with immediate protective orders.

This protects genuinely endangered parties while requiring evidence to prevent strategic false accusations.

The system has to be able to distinguish between “this is hard and I’m unhappy” and “this is genuinely dangerous.” Current system treats everything the same; better system makes that distinction.

7. Economic Fairness Without Strategic Rewards

Neither party should be financially destroyed by divorce, but strategic divorce shouldn’t be financially rewarded either.

Child support based on actual costs of raising children, not lifestyle maintenance or punitive amounts. Equal custody presumption means both parents contribute proportionally to their income.

Alimony limited to transition period (2-3 years) except in extreme cases (e.g., spouse disabled by caring for children for decades). The goal is helping someone get on their feet, not permanent wealth transfer.

Asset division should be equitable but account for fault. If one spouse’s documented behavior destroyed the marriage, that should affect the split.

8. Children’s Attachment Interests Paramount

Every custody and visitation decision should optimize for minimizing attachment disruption to children.

Primary question: which arrangement provides the most stability and consistency for children’s attachment relationships?

Not “which parent is better” (creates adversarial incentives) but “what arrangement least disrupts children’s existing attachment bonds?”

This means: - Young children (0-5) shouldn’t shuttle between homes frequently - Maintaining relationships with both parents is critical - Stability and predictability matter more than perfect equality of time - Children’s developmental needs outweigh parents’ preferences

Why This Won’t Happen

Everything described above would measurably improve outcomes for children while still allowing adults to exit genuinely failed marriages.

It won’t be implemented because concentrated interests prevent it:

Family law attorneys profit from contentious divorce. Cooperative mediation and presumptive equal custody would eliminate most of their billable hours. The adversarial custody battle is where they make money.

Courts profit from drawn-out proceedings. Simplified cooperative divorce with mediation reduces court load and judicial power. Family courts have institutional interests in maintaining complexity.

Therapeutic industry profits from broken families. Millions of therapists, counselors, and social workers depend on the steady stream of attachment-disordered adults and children of divorce. Reducing divorce and its damage reduces their client base.

Ideological capture prevents honest discussion. Progressives won’t accept any barriers to unilateral exit because it feels like restricting women’s autonomy. Conservatives won’t accept equal custody presumptions because they’re invested in traditional maternal custody. Neither side will optimize for children’s attachment outcomes if it conflicts with their ideological commitments.

Public choice theory wins. Concentrated interests (lawyers, courts, therapeutic industry) have strong incentives to maintain the current system. Diffuse interests (better outcomes for children, healthier adult relationships, lower societal dysfunction) have weak lobbying power.

This is the same dynamic that prevents economic reform, healthcare reform, or any other change where concentrated interests profit from dysfunction.

What This Means Practically

If the system won’t reform—and it almost certainly won’t without civilizational collapse forcing a reset—then what?

For individuals:

Don’t marry or have children unless you’re extremely confident in: - Your partner’s attachment security (securely attached people have much lower divorce rates) - Your partner’s character over time (this is where community vetting matters) - Your own ability to navigate conflict without weaponizing power - Your willingness to stay even when it’s hard

The system no longer protects you or your children. Marriage is higher risk than it’s ever been in modern history. Act accordingly.

For communities:

Build parallel structures that make the principles above possible: - Long-term character assessment of members (reputation systems) - Community mediation infrastructure - Support for struggling marriages before they reach crisis - Social consequences for abandoning marriages with young children - Financial and practical support making single-income families viable again

Early Christians survived Rome’s collapse by building mutual aid networks, stable family structures, and community accountability when the Empire’s systems failed. The same strategy applies now.

For society:

We probably need collapse before reconstruction becomes possible. The interests profiting from family breakdown are too entrenched. The ideological commitments preventing honest discussion about tradeoffs are too strong.

What comes after collapse will depend on whether anyone preserved the knowledge of what actually works—what attachment security requires, what children’s developmental needs are, what coordination mechanisms make stable family formation possible.

The Uncomfortable Truth

No-fault divorce liberated adults from bad marriages by creating an attachment disorder factory that damaged millions of children and will continue damaging their children.

We can’t go back to trapping people in marriages. But we also can’t continue destroying children’s attachment security at scale while pretending this is progress.

A better system is possible. It would: - Allow exit when genuinely necessary - Protect children’s attachment development during critical periods - Remove strategic incentives from custody and divorce - Create accountability for destructive behavior - Support struggling marriages before they fail

But that system requires accepting tradeoffs—some restrictions on unilateral exit, some community involvement in private decisions, some barriers that feel uncomfortable to modern individualism.

The concentrated interests profiting from the current system won’t allow reform. The ideological commitments on both sides won’t allow honest discussion of tradeoffs.

So we’ll continue producing generations of attachment-damaged children, watching relationship formation collapse, and pretending this is fine because at least adults can exit marriages easily.

The children who didn’t choose any of this will pay the price for our inability to face uncomfortable truths.
